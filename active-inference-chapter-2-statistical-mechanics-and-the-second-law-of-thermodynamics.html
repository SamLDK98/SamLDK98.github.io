Classical mechanics works for systems with a few objects and trajectories: a planet orbiting the sun or the flight of an arrow.

But for systems containing billions of interacting particles, tracking every trajectory is impossible.

Fortunately, statistical mechanics introduced a new approach.

Before we continue, it is worth briefly explaining what we mean by systems and states.

A system is simply a set of things that may vary in some way.

At any one moment in time, these variables will have specific values. We call the set of these values a state of the system.

Imagine a 4x4 grid with 8 dots that randomly move around it.

The system is the collection of dots, and the way they vary is by their spatial location on a grid.

Microstates are specific configurations of these dots.

With 16 cells and 8 dots, there are 12,870 possible unique microstates.

Now, imagine a device that measures how many dots are on either the left or right of the grid in each microstate.

Depending on this balance, the device displays a colour. We call the colours macroscopic observables.

The macrostate is the set of all configurations that produce the same macroscopic observable/measurement (in this case colour).

Entropy measures how many different microscopic ways a macroscopic state can exist.

The macroscopic observables define the range of microscopic configurations within each set.

The partitioning of microstate space into fewer macrostates is called coarse-graining.

Notice how different macrostates contain different numbers of compatible microstates.

If the dots move randomly around the grid, you can see that the higher-entropy macrostates occur more frequently because they correspond to more compatible microstates.

Blue has the highest entropy and is most often displayed.

At this small scale, fluctuations between macrostates occur frequently.

But let's scale everything up. Consider a grid of 80 million cells with 40 million dots. Our measuring device still detects the same balance ratios across the left and right sides.

As the size of the system increases, even tiny deviations from equilibrium become astronomically unlikely.

A 1% imbalance (51:49) lies about 126 standard deviations from equilibrium.

The probability of observing such a fluctuation, or anything more extreme, is roughly 10-3477.

The probability is so small that, even if every atom in the observable universe represented a new randomly sampled microstate every second since the beginning of time, the fluctuation would still almost certainly never occur.

But why does the drift toward equilibrium even happen in the first place?

Move all 8 dots to the left side of the grid. Notice that there is literally no way for the next move to make the distribution more concentrated on the left; every available move spreads things out.

Once a dot moves, the system enters a region of state space containing more compatible microstates.

This statistical tendency is the explanation for why systems drift toward higher-entropy macrostates.

The system is not trying to maximize entropy. It simply wanders randomly through microstates, and overwhelmingly many of those microstates belong to high-entropy macrostates.

So why does heat drift from hot to cold?

The system is all the particles in the room.

The current microstate belongs to a low-entropy macrostate in which a disproportionate amount of thermal energy is concentrated within one region of the system.

There are overwhelmingly more microscopic configurations in which this energy is dispersed throughout the surrounding air than concentrated within the coffee alone.

As the system moves between microstates, it therefore overwhelmingly tends toward equilibrium.

But this immediately raised a deeper problem.

Living systems do not appear to drift passively toward equilibrium.

Cells maintain boundaries. Organisms preserve highly specific internal structure. Brains sustain coordinated electrical activity. Ecosystems maintain dynamic organization over enormous spans of time.

If entropy tends to increase, why do living systems not simply decay into equilibrium like everything else?
