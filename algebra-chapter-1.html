Algebra · Chapter the First

The Unknown

"When I consider what people generally want in calculating, I found that it always is a number." — al-Khwārizmī

Before there was an x, there was a thing with no name.

Merchants knew it well. An inheritance to be divided, a debt to be settled, a plot of land to be measured -- and somewhere in the middle of the problem, a number nobody knew yet. The whole calculation circled around a blank.

This chapter is about the discovery that the blank could be treated as a number anyway -- moved, weighed, balanced against the knowns, and finally cornered until it confessed its value.

That discovery has an address and a date.

The House of Wisdom

In the early ninth century, the caliph al-Ma'mūn gathered astronomers, translators, and mathematicians into a great library in Baghdad -- the House of Wisdom. Among them was a scholar named Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī.

Around the year 820 he wrote a book with a very practical aim: to teach the calculations "people constantly require" -- in inheritance, trade, surveying, and the digging of canals. Its title contained two words that named the method:

al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wa-l-muqābala

The Compendious Book on Calculation by Restoration and Balancing

Restoration and Balancing

al-jabr — "the restoring of broken parts." In old Spain an algebrista also set broken bones.The first word, al-jabr, means restoration: when a quantity is subtracted on one side of an equation, restore it -- make it whole -- by adding it to both sides. A deficit is mended like a broken bone.

al-muqābala — "the facing of two things, one against the other."The second, al-muqābala, means balancing: when the same quantity stands on both sides, strike it from each, until nothing is left that appears twice.

Two moves. Restore what is broken; cancel what is doubled. Every step keeps the two sides in balance, like pans of a scale -- and step by step, the unknown is cornered.

Centuries later, Europe would take the first of those two words, al-jabr, and wear it down into a name for the whole subject: algebra. And when al-Khwārizmī's own name was Latinised -- Algoritmi -- it became our word for a step-by-step procedure: algorithm. One man, two words, both still in daily use.

An Equation Without Symbols

Here is the remarkable part: the book contains no symbols at all. No x, no equals sign, no plus. Every problem is a sentence. The most famous reads:

"A square and ten roots are equal to thirty-nine dirhams."

A "square" is the unknown multiplied by itself. A "root" is the unknown. In the symbols we use today -- symbols that would not exist for another eight hundred years --

x² + 10x = 39

And the balance is not a metaphor. It is the whole engine:

Whatever is done to one pan must be done to the other -- that is the entire law. Restore, balance, and repeat, and the sentence slowly resolves until the square stands alone and its root can be read off. (Al-Khwārizmī's answer: the root is 3 -- and his method for finding it, completing the square, is a story for the next chapter.)

The Long Road to x

x, first pressed into this service in Descartes' La Géométrie, 1637.For eight centuries algebra was written in sentences -- rhetorical algebra, problem after problem spelled out in prose. Symbols arrived slowly, and from many hands: signs for plus and minus, a sign for equality, and at last, in 1637, a Frenchman reaching for the last letters of the alphabet to stand for the unknowns.

The blank in the merchant's problem finally had a name: x.

But the name is the smallest part. The deep idea is al-Khwārizmī's: the unknown obeys the same arithmetic as the known. You can add it, scale it, weigh it in the pans -- without ever knowing what it is. The rest of this treatise is what follows from taking that idea seriously.
