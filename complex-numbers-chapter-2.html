Complex Numbers · Chapter 2

The Complex Plane

Powers of $i$

Most real numbers, when raised to successive powers, either grow larger in magnitude or shrink towards zero.

There are, however, two real numbers that exhibit a different behaviour.

$1^n$ always remains $1$.

$(-1)^n$ cycles repeatedly between $-1$ and $1$.

What do you think happens to $i$?

If:

$$i^2 = -1$$

Then:

$$i^3 = i^2 \cdot i = -i$$

And:

$$i^4 = (i^2)^2 = (-1)^2 = 1$$

And:

$$i^5 = i^4 \cdot i = i$$

Have a look at what's happening.

$$ \begin{array}{l | l} 2^1 = 2 & i^1 = i \\ 2^2 = 4 & i^2 = -1 \\ 2^3 = 8 & i^3 = -i \\ 2^4 = 16 & i^4 = 1 \\ 2^5 = 32 & i^5 = i \\ 2^6 = 64 & i^6 = -1 \\ 2^7 = 128 & i^7 = -i \\ 2^8 = 256 & i^8 = 1 \\ \end{array} $$

Similar to $(-1)^n$, we see cyclical behaviour. This time, however, it cycles between four values:

$$i, -1, -i, 1$$

Rotation on the Number Line

Let's think about multiplication and the number line.

  • Multiplying by a positive number changes magnitude but preserves direction.
  • Multiplying by a negative number changes magnitude and direction.
  • Multiplying by $-1$ reverses direction on the number line, but preserves magnitude.

Geometrically, multiplying by $-1$ can be thought of as a $180^\circ$ rotation about the origin of the number line.

So multiplication by $-1$ corresponds to a $180^\circ$ rotation. But could there be a number that performs only a $90^\circ$ rotation?

Well,

$$i^2=-1$$

Therefore, two successive multiplications by $i$ must have the same overall effect as one multiplication by $-1$.

Since multiplication by $-1$ corresponds to a $180^\circ$ rotation, multiplication by $i$ must correspond to half of that: a $90^\circ$ rotation.

But there is a problem. A $90^\circ$ rotation would move us off the number line entirely.

To represent such a rotation, we need a second axis.

We can let our number line be the horizontal axis. It contains the real numbers, so let's call it the real axis.

This new vertical axis we will call the imaginary axis.

Starting at $1$:

$$1 \xrightarrow{\times i} i$$

A second multiplication gives:

$$i^2=-1$$

A third gives:

$$i^3=-i$$

A fourth gives:

$$i^4=1$$

Each multiplication by $i$ rotates the number by a quarter-turn:

$$1 \rightarrow i \rightarrow -1 \rightarrow -i \rightarrow 1$$

The Complex Plane

So $i$ is not somewhere further left or right on the number line—it points in a new direction.

This plane, comprised of the real and imaginary axes, is called the complex plane.

We can describe a number by saying how far it moves in each direction.

For example:

$$3 + 5i$$

means:

  • move $3$ units in the real direction
  • move $5$ units in the imaginary direction

This gives us a new kind of number with two components:

$$z=a + bi$$

Numbers of this form are called complex numbers.

Here, complex is used in its older sense of composed of parts:

  • A real part ($a$)
  • An imaginary part ($b$)
  • An imaginary component ($bi$)

Recap

We have introduced a new number $i$, defined by this strange property that it squares to $-1$.

Then we saw that we could combine it with real numbers to produce the square root of any negative number. These are called imaginary numbers.

$$3i=\sqrt{ -9 } \qquad 5i = \sqrt{ -25 } \qquad 12i=\sqrt{ -144 }$$

Addition, however, appears to break down: we can't add $i$ to a real number. The expression cannot be simplified into a single real number. The real part and imaginary part are unlike terms, just like in the expression $3 +5x$.

$$3 + 5i$$

However, the key realisation is that we are not simply extending the number line, but creating a two-dimensional number system. The fact that the real and imaginary parts can't be added actually becomes a feature.

We can consider each part to represent movement along an independent axis. Together they form the complex plane.
