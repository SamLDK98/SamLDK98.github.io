Linear Algebra

Span

Span refers to the regions of space reachable by linear combinations (scaled and added) of basis vectors.

In other words, what outputs can our system reach?

Span is determined by how many independent vectors we have (i.e. vectors pointing in different directions), and limited by the dimensions of the output space.

We can get a sense of our potential span when looking at a matrix:

$$ A= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1\\ 2 & -1\\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} $$

  • The number of columns tells us we have 2 input vectors.
  • The number of rows tells us we have three equations (3-dimensional output vectors).

So the columns can at most span a 2-dimensional region inside a 3-dimensional output space, if they are independent. We call such a system 'overdetermined'.

Overdetermined systems

  • More equations than independent input vectors, i.e. more rows than columns.
  • The output space is of a higher dimension than what we can span with our input vectors.
  • For example, we might span a plane inside a 3D space.
  • Many potential target vectors are unreachable.

Underdetermined systems

  • More independent input vectors than equations, i.e. more columns than rows.
  • Now, infinitely many input vectors can produce the same output.
  • Often there are infinitely many solutions.

Square systems

A square system has the same number of inputs and outputs.

If the basis directions are independent, the transformation can reach every point in the output space and be reversed uniquely.
