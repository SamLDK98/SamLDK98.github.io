Complex Numbers · Chapter 1

Complex Numbers

We now have two completed stories.

The geometric branch gave us circles, sine and cosine as coordinates, and the discovery that rotation produces oscillation.

The algebra branch gave us exponents, continuous growth, the number $e$, and an infinite series for $e^x$.

These stories appear to have nothing in common. One is about turning; the other is about growing.

This chapter builds the bridge between them. The bridge is made of a new kind of number.

Numbers That Answer Impossible Questions

The number system has been stretched before. Each time, the stretching was provoked by a question the existing numbers could not answer.

What is $3 - 5$? No counting number answers that. The negatives were invented -- and dismissed by many mathematicians of the time as absurd.

What is $3 \div 5$? No whole number answers that. Fractions were admitted.

Each new layer seemed suspicious at first, then became indispensable.

Here is the question that provoked the next stretch:

$$x^2 = -1$$

What number, multiplied by itself, gives $-1$?

A positive times a positive is positive. A negative times a negative is also positive. Zero gives zero. Every number on the number line fails.

The historical response was the same move as before: rather than declaring the question impossible, invent a number that answers it, and see whether arithmetic survives.

The new number is written $i$, and it is defined by one property alone:

$$i^2 = -1$$

Arithmetic survives. Everything works. The only casualty was the name: sceptics called $i$ "imaginary", and the insult stuck as terminology.

For now, $i^2 = -1$ has to be taken on faith as a rule of the game. By the middle of this chapter, you will see why it is true.

A Number With Two Parts

Once $i$ exists, it starts combining with ordinary numbers. We can scale it ($2i$, $-\frac{1}{2}i$) and add it to ordinary numbers:

$$z = 3 + 2i$$

Notice that this sum cannot be simplified any further. Three of one thing plus two of another -- like 3 apples and 2 oranges -- the parts stay separate.

So this new kind of number permanently carries two independent components: an ordinary part and an $i$ part. Numbers like this are called complex numbers -- "complex" in the old sense of composed of parts, not in the sense of complicated.

But this raises a question. Ordinary numbers live on the number line. Every position is already taken.

Where does $i$ live?

Not at 0 -- it isn't nothing. Not at 1 or $-1$ -- squaring those gives $+1$. There is no room anywhere on the line.

So it lives off the line.

Give the imaginary numbers their own axis, perpendicular to the real one, crossing at zero. The real numbers run east-west; the imaginary numbers run north-south.

And now every complex number $a + bi$ has a home: walk $a$ along the real axis, then $b$ up the imaginary one.

A complex number is a point in a plane.

Or, often more usefully: an arrow from the origin to that point.

Length and Direction

If a complex number is an arrow, then it has the two properties every arrow has: a length and a direction.

These should feel familiar. They are exactly the quantities we spent the geometric branch studying.

The length comes from Pythagoras -- the real and imaginary parts are the legs of a right triangle:

$$r = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$$

The direction is an angle $\theta$, measured anticlockwise from the positive real axis.

And from the geometric branch, we know how to recover the legs from $r$ and $\theta$:

$$a = r\cos\theta \qquad b = r\sin\theta$$

Substituting these into $z = a + bi$ and factoring out the $r$:

$$z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$$

We derived this expression at the end of the Polar Form chapter, where it may have felt like a curiosity. Look who is standing inside it now: cosine and sine, the coordinates of a point on a circle, living in the middle of a number.

Every complex number can be described in two interchangeable ways: by its parts $(a, b)$, or by its length and direction $(r, \theta)$. Toggle between them in the widget above.

What Multiplication Does

Adding complex numbers is unremarkable: add the real parts, add the imaginary parts.

Multiplication is where the magic is. Let's sneak up on it by multiplying the simplest things we can.

Start with the number 1: an arrow of length 1 pointing east. Multiply it by $i$:

$$1 \cdot i = i$$

The arrow now points north. Multiplying by $i$ turned it a quarter-circle anticlockwise.

Multiply by $i$ again:

$$i \cdot i = i^2 = -1$$

North has become west. Another quarter-turn.

Again: $-1 \cdot i = -i$. West to south. Again: $-i \cdot i = -i^2 = 1$. South back to east. Home.

Multiplying by $i$ is a $90^\circ$ rotation.

And now $i^2 = -1$ stops being mysterious.

Multiplying by $-1$ flips a number to the opposite side of zero -- it points the arrow backwards. Pointing backwards is a $180^\circ$ rotation.

So the question "what number squares to $-1$?" is really asking:

"What operation, done twice, makes a half-turn?"

A quarter-turn. $i$ is not a strange quantity hiding somewhere on the number line. It is the halfway point of a flip -- and it was invisible for centuries because everyone was looking on the line, where no halfway point exists.

Notice also what the widget shows about repetition: each press of "$\times\, i$" adds another quarter-turn. Repeated multiplication is repeated rotation. Four turns bring you home, which is why the powers of $i$ cycle forever:

$$i^1 = i \qquad i^2 = -1 \qquad i^3 = -i \qquad i^4 = 1 \qquad i^5 = i \;\; \ldots$$

Multiplying Any Two Complex Numbers

Multiplying by $i$ rotates by $90^\circ$ exactly. What about multiplying by a general complex number $w$?

Here is the claim, and it is the most important fact in this chapter:

Multiplying by $w$ does two things at once. It rotates by $w$'s angle, and it scales by $w$'s length.

Why should that be? Think about what $w = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$ is made of. The real part says "keep some of the original direction". The imaginary part says "mix in some of the quarter-turned version". Blending an arrow with its own quarter-turned copy tilts it part-way -- a rotation by something between $0^\circ$ and $90^\circ$. The proportions of the blend set the tilt; the overall size of $w$ sets the stretch.

Let's verify it on an example where we can check both ways. Take $z = 1 + i$ and square it.

The algebraic way -- expand, and use $i^2 = -1$:

$$(1+i)(1+i) = 1 + i + i + i^2 = 1 + 2i - 1 = 2i$$

The geometric way: $1+i$ sits at $45^\circ$ with length $\sqrt{2}$. Multiplying it by itself should rotate $45^\circ$ by another $45^\circ$ (giving $90^\circ$) and scale $\sqrt{2}$ by another $\sqrt{2}$ (giving 2).

An arrow of length 2 pointing straight up is... $2i$. The two answers agree.

The algebra grinds it out symbol by symbol. The geometry sees it. Drag the arrows below and watch the rule hold everywhere:

In summary, when complex numbers multiply:

$$\text{angles } \textbf{add} \qquad \text{magnitudes } \textbf{multiply}$$

An Echo From the Algebra Branch

Read that summary line again, slowly. Multiplying the numbers... adds the angles.

We have met behaviour like this before. It was the very first thing we learned in the algebra branch:

$$a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$$

Multiplying powers adds the exponents. Exponents turn multiplication into addition.

And here, on the complex plane: multiplying rotations adds the angles. Angles are behaving exactly like exponents.

Hold that thought. It is the loose thread the final chapter will pull.

The Circle, Packaged Into a Number

Among all complex numbers, the ones with length exactly 1 deserve special attention. Multiplying by them doesn't stretch anything -- it only rotates.

Where do they live? At distance 1 from the origin: on the unit circle.

Our old friend has reappeared, and in this setting every point on it is a pure rotation, waiting to be applied:

$$\cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

This is worth pausing on. In the geometric branch, a point on the unit circle was a pair of coordinates, $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$, and rotating it meant fussy bookkeeping with two components.

Now the pair has been packaged into a single number -- and rotation has become ordinary multiplication. Because angles add, rotating by $\theta$ and then by $\varphi$ is one clean statement:

$$(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)(\cos\varphi + i\sin\varphi) = \cos(\theta+\varphi) + i\sin(\theta+\varphi)$$

No triangle diagrams, no coordinate juggling. The arithmetic of complex numbers does the geometry for us. This is why they are so useful: they are the natural language of rotation.

Oscillation, Revisited

In the Waves chapter, a point circling at $f$ revolutions per second had coordinates that oscillated: the $x$-coordinate traced a cosine wave, the $y$-coordinate a sine wave.

In our new language, that circling point is a single rotating complex number:

$$z(t) = \cos(2\pi f t) + i\sin(2\pi f t)$$

Its real part is the cosine wave. Its imaginary part is the sine wave.

Think of the rotating arrow casting shadows: one onto the real axis, one onto the imaginary axis. Each shadow bobs back and forth as the arrow turns.

An oscillation is the shadow of a rotation.

This is more than a pretty picture. The wave -- the thing we measure in sound, light, and current -- is one component of something simpler: steady, uniform rotation. Working with the rotation and taking the shadow at the end is often far easier than wrestling with the wave itself. This single idea underlies a large share of physics and engineering.

A Suspicious Resemblance

Let's gather the clues this chapter has produced.

Rotations compose by adding angles. Powers compose by adding exponents. So if rotation by $\theta$ could be written as a power -- some base raised to $\theta$ -- the bookkeeping would match perfectly:

$$R^{\theta_1} \cdot R^{\theta_2} = R^{\theta_1+\theta_2}$$

But raised to what base? For that, recall the algebra branch.

$e^x$ is continuous growth: at every instant, the quantity grows in proportion to itself -- it grows in the direction it already points, which is why it races off along the number line.

Now ask a strange question. What if, at every instant, the growth were turned sideways -- pushed at $90^\circ$ to the direction the arrow currently points?

Pushing sideways never lengthens an arrow. It only changes where it points. An arrow forever nudged at right angles to itself doesn't grow at all -- it travels in a circle.

And we have just spent a chapter with the number whose entire job is to turn things $90^\circ$: the number $i$.

So here is a daring guess. Continuous growth is $e^x$. Continuous growth turned sideways -- growth in the $i$ direction -- ought to be:

$$e^{i\theta}$$

If the guess is right, $e^{i\theta}$ doesn't grow at all. It rotates. After turning through an angle $\theta$, it should be sitting on the unit circle at exactly the point we have learned to call:

$$\cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

Which would mean that these two expressions -- one from the algebra branch, one from the geometric branch -- are the same thing.

That is an extraordinary claim, and we have not earned it yet. A guess built on intuition needs to survive contact with something solid.

Fortunately, we own something solid: an infinite series for $e^x$ -- powers over factorials -- that works for every input. Nothing stops us from feeding it $i\theta$ and grinding out what comes back.

That calculation is the next chapter. The two branches are about to meet.
