Trigonometry

Waves

In the previous chapters we made an unexpected discovery. A point moving steadily around a circle, viewed through its coordinates, traces out a wave.

In this chapter we take that wave seriously as an object in its own right.

What Is a Wave?

Waves are everywhere. Sound is a wave in air pressure. Light is a wave in electric and magnetic fields. There are water waves, radio waves, the alternating current in your walls, and the rhythm of your own heartbeat.

These seem like very different things. What do they have in common?

A wave is a pattern that repeats.

Some waves travel through a medium, and physicists sort these by how the medium moves.

In a transverse wave, each point of the medium moves at right angles to the direction the wave travels. A ripple crosses a pond horizontally, but the water itself only bobs up and down.

In a longitudinal wave, each point moves back and forth along the direction of travel. Sound squeezes and stretches the air in the same direction it is heading.

And some waves don't travel anywhere at all. A pendulum swings, a weight on a spring bounces, a heart beats. Nothing moves from place to place. The pattern repeats purely in time. We call this an oscillation.

While switching between the three motions above, keep your eye on the bottom panel.

The rope point bobs vertically. The air molecule shuffles horizontally. The spring bounces in place. Three different motions.

Yet the displacement of a single point, tracked over time, traces out the same shape in all three cases.

That shape is the sine wave.

This is why trigonometry, a subject that began with triangles, ends up describing sound, light, and electricity. Learn to describe this one shape, and you have a language for all of them.

From Angle to Time

There is a problem, though.

Sine takes an angle as input.

$$\sin(\theta)$$

But a wave unfolds in time. To describe one, we need a function that takes a time as input.

The unit circle provides the bridge.

Imagine the point moving around the circle at a steady speed, completing exactly one revolution per second.

After $t$ seconds, it has completed $t$ revolutions.

One revolution is $2\pi$ radians, so after $t$ seconds the angle is:

$$\theta = 2\pi t$$

Substituting this into sine:

$$x(t) = \sin(2\pi t)$$

Sine of an angle has become sine of a time.

The input $t$ is a number of seconds. The output $x(t)$ is the height of the point on the circle: a number between $-1$ and $1$ that rises and falls forever as $t$ increases.

Frequency and Period

There is no reason the point must complete exactly one revolution per second. It could spin faster, or slower.

The number of revolutions completed per second is called the frequency, written $f$. It is measured in hertz (Hz), where $1$ Hz means one full cycle per second.

A point spinning at $f$ revolutions per second has completed $ft$ revolutions after $t$ seconds, so the angle becomes:

$$\theta = 2\pi f t$$

We could also ask the reverse question. Rather than "how many cycles fit into one second?", we could ask, "how much time does one cycle take?"

This is called the period, written $T$, and measured in seconds.

Frequency and period are two descriptions of the same fact, so each determines the other. If a wave completes 2 cycles every second, then each cycle must take half a second.

$$T = \frac{1}{f} \qquad \qquad f = \frac{1}{T}$$

They are reciprocals. As one goes up, the other comes down.

Amplitude

On the unit circle the radius is 1, so the height of the point ranges between $-1$ and $1$.

Real waves come in different sizes. A whisper and a shout are both pressure waves with the same shape, but one displaces the air far more than the other.

To change the size of the wave, we change the size of the circle.

If the radius is $A$ instead of 1, every height gets scaled by $A$:

$$x(t) = A\sin(2\pi f t)$$

$A$ is called the amplitude: the maximum displacement from rest.

It stretches the wave vertically without affecting its timing.

Phase

Two waves can have the same frequency and the same amplitude, and still fail to line up.

How? One of them started at a different point in its cycle.

On the circle, this corresponds to the point beginning its journey at some angle other than $0$.

We call this starting angle the phase, written $\varphi$ ("phi"). It is added to the angle inside sine:

$$x(t) = A\sin(2\pi f t + \varphi)$$

Phase slides the wave horizontally without affecting its size or its speed.

Phase also reveals the true relationship between sine and cosine.

Cosine is just sine with a quarter-turn head start:

$$\cos(\theta) = \sin\!\left(\theta + \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$$

They are the same wave, out of phase by a quarter of a cycle.

Interpreting $x(t)=\sin(2\pi ft)$

Let's return to the expression at the heart of this chapter.

$$x(t) = \sin(2\pi f t)$$

Expressions like this appear constantly in physics, engineering, and signal processing, so it is worth being able to read them fluently.

The trick is to read from the inside out.

$t$ is the time, in seconds.

$ft$ answers the question: spinning at $f$ revolutions per second, how many revolutions have been completed after $t$ seconds?

$2\pi f t$ converts those revolutions into an angle, because one revolution is $2\pi$ radians.

$\sin(2\pi f t)$ looks up the height of the point on the unit circle at that angle.

So the whole expression says: "Spin around the unit circle $f$ times per second. $x(t)$ is your height at time $t$."

Let's check this with $f = 2$ Hz at time $t = \frac{1}{4}$ s.

After a quarter of a second, spinning twice per second, the point has completed $ft = \frac{1}{2}$ a revolution.

Half a revolution is an angle of $\pi$, which lands the point at $(-1, 0)$, on the $x$-axis.

Its height there is 0, so:

$$\sin\!\left(2\pi \cdot 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{4}\right) = \sin(\pi) = 0$$

With just three numbers -- an amplitude, a frequency, and a phase -- we can now describe any pure wave:

$$x(t) = A\sin(2\pi f t + \varphi)$$

Remarkably, more complicated waves -- a violin note, a heartbeat, a voice -- turn out to be sums of these pure waves.

How that works is a story for another chapter.
