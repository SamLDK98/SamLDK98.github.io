a) The Problem of Irreversibility

Newton's laws of motion make no distinction between past and future. Anything that can happen in one direction may happen just the same in the reverse.

That does not mean Newton's equations predict apples should spontaneously rise from the ground. It means that if every particle involved in the process had its motion reversed perfectly, the equations would describe the reversed trajectory just as consistently as the forward one.

Newton was probably sharp enough to notice that people grow old, not young, and tomorrow is never yesterday; however, this direction of change over time was simply considered unrelated to the fabric of motion and matter.

However, once heat was understood as arising from the microscopic motion of matter, it became impossible to keep irreversible change outside of physics.

The emerging paradox was:

  • At the macroscopic, observable level, heat appears to flow in only one direction: from hotter to colder regions.
  • But heat arises from motion and matter, and the laws of motion and matter do not describe a specific direction.

So: if Newton's laws governed the microscopic motion of particles, and those laws made no distinction between past and future, why did macroscopic, observable systems appear to evolve irreversibly?

b) Statistical Mechanics

To understand why heat disperses from hotter regions to colder ones, we first need to understand entropy and why it tends to increase over time.

We will first consider a very abstract example.

Imagine a 4×4 grid with 8 dots that randomly move around it.

Any specific configuration of these dots across the cells is a microstate of the system. With 8 dots in 16 cells, there are 12,870 unique microstates.

Now imagine the dots carry heat with them. Regions containing more dots are therefore warmer than regions containing fewer.

The resulting distribution of heat is a large-scale property emerging from the microscopic arrangement of the dots. We call such large-scale properties macroscopic observables.

Rather than tracking the exact position of every dot, we may instead describe the system in broader terms such as:

  • “hotter on the left,”
  • “hotter on the right,”
  • or “roughly equal temperature throughout.”

Each such macroscopic description defines a macrostate.

A macrostate is therefore the set of all microstates compatible with some observable large-scale description of the system.

A macrostate may therefore be understood in two equivalent ways:

  • as a large-scale observable description of the system,
  • or as the set of all microstates compatible with that description.

Different macrostates contain different numbers of compatible microstates.

If a macrostate is defined as all the dots being on the left-hand side, then there is only one compatible microstate.

If instead there must be four dots on the left and four on the right, there are 4,900 possible microstates compatible with that macrostate.

If the dots wander randomly, we'd expect to see an even temperature distribution most of the time, with occasional fluctuations into warmer/colder regions left or right.

If our system begins near equilibrium, large deviations away from that equilibrium become astronomically unlikely.

Even a 1% imbalance (245,000 dots on one side and 255,000 on the other) lies about twenty standard deviations from equilibrium.

The probability of such a fluctuation, or anything more extreme, is around 10-89.

Perturbation

Let's imagine we introduce 150K new dots to the left side. The macrostate would likely change; there would be a hotter left and colder right.

But over time the system would appear to drift toward equilibrium, and then stay there.

But why?

Return to the 4×4 grid. If all 8 dots start on the left side (filling columns 1 and 2), the 4 dots in column 1 are completely boxed in: surrounded by other dots and the edge.

The 4 dots in column 2 can only move in one direction: right, into the empty half.

There is literally no way for the next move to make the grid more concentrated on the left; every available move spreads things out.

When a system is in a low-multiplicity macrostate, the overwhelming majority of available moves take it toward higher-multiplicity macrostates, simply because there are more of those to move into.

There are simply far more ways for the system to become balanced than remain uneven.

Entropy is a measure of how many microstates are compatible with a macrostate. More precisely, it is proportional to the logarithm of that count: S = k·ln(Ω). The log doesn't change the qualitative picture; it just keeps the numbers manageable when Ω is astronomical.

For example, our system has low entropy when all the dots are confined to one side, because even the movement of one dot will change the macrostate.

Other real-world examples:

High entropy -- a cloud of gas: There are trillions of ways the molecules can fly around and still look like a “cloud.”

Low entropy -- diamond crystal: The atoms must be in very specific spots. If you move one, the structure changes.

You'll often hear that systems “try” to maximize entropy. This is misleading. The system isn't trying to do anything. It wanders randomly through microstates, and overwhelmingly many of those microstates belong to high-entropy macrostates.

Macroscopic irreversibility emerges statistically rather than being explicitly built into the microscopic laws themselves.

A final note:

This analogy uses spatial position, but entropy isn't specifically about geometry. The same logic applies to any system with a meaningful distinction between microscopic configurations and macroscopic observables: the velocities of gas molecules, the alignments of magnetic spins, the energy levels of an ensemble. What matters is the multiplicity, not what the dots represent.

Statistical mechanics therefore appeared to explain why physical systems drift toward equilibrium. Heat disperses not because microscopic laws contain a preferred direction in time, but because overwhelmingly more microscopic arrangements correspond to dispersed states than concentrated ones.

But this immediately raised a deeper problem.

Living systems do not appear to drift passively toward equilibrium.

Cells maintain boundaries. Organisms preserve highly specific internal structure. Brains sustain coordinated electrical activity. Ecosystems maintain dynamic organization over enormous spans of time.

If entropy tends to increase, why do living systems not simply decay into equilibrium like everything else?
