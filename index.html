a) The Problem of Irreversibility

Newton's laws of motion make no distinction between past and future. Anything that can happen in one direction may happen just the same in the reverse.

That does not mean Newton's equations predict apples should spontaneously rise from the ground. It means that if every particle involved in the process had its motion reversed perfectly, the equations would describe the reversed trajectory just as consistently as the forward one.

Newton was probably sharp enough to notice that people grow old, not young, and tomorrow is never yesterday; however, this direction of change over time was simply considered unrelated to the fabric of motion and matter.

However, once heat was understood as arising from the microscopic motion of matter, it became impossible to keep irreversible change outside of physics.

The emerging paradox was:

  • At the macroscopic, observable level, heat appears to flow in only one direction: from hotter to colder regions.
  • But heat arises from motion and matter, and the laws of motion and matter do not describe a specific direction.

So: if Newton's laws governed the microscopic motion of particles, and those laws made no distinction between past and future, why did macroscopic, observable systems appear to evolve irreversibly?

b) Statistical Mechanics

Classical mechanics works for systems with a few objects and trajectories: a planet orbiting the sun or the flight of an arrow.

But for systems containing billions of interacting particles, tracking every trajectory is impossible.

Fortunately, statistical mechanics introduced a new approach.

Before we continue, it is worth briefly explaining what we mean by systems and states.

A system is simply a set of things that may vary in some way.

At any one moment in time, those variables will have specific values. We call this a state of the system.

As time passes, the system evolves, moving between different states.

Looking at the distribution of possible states provides the key insight.

Some states occur more frequently than others.

Let's take a simple, abstract example to illustrate.

Imagine a 4x4 grid with 8 dots that randomly move around it.

Imagine the dots carry heat.

Notice how different macrostates contain different numbers of compatible microstates.

If the dots wander randomly, we'd expect to see an even temperature distribution most of the time, with occasional fluctuations into warmer/colder regions left or right.

At this small scale those fluctuations happen often enough to be noticeable.

If our system begins near equilibrium, large deviations away from that equilibrium become astronomically unlikely.

Even a 1% imbalance (245,000 dots on one side and 255,000 on the other) lies about twenty standard deviations from equilibrium.

The probability of such a fluctuation, or anything more extreme, is around 10-89.
