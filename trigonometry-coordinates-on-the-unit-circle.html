Limitations beyond ordered pairs

Previously, we looked at equations like $4x-2y=2$.

We said that $x$ or $y$ could be anything, as long as the other adjusted accordingly to balance the equation. So, we were limited to pairings $(x,y)$, not either variable alone.

However, some equations fundamentally limit what $x$ and/or $y$ can be.

For example, can you think of an $x$ value above or below 1 that can be balanced by some $y$ value to make this equation true?

$$x^2 + y^2 =1$$

Let's try one for example:

$$5^2 + y^2 = 1$$

$$25+y^2=1 \to y^2=-24 \to \boxed{y = \sqrt{ -24 }}$$

Now -- you might be wondering, "So what if it's the square root of a negative? Why must it be a real number?". And you would be right to push on this. In fact, it was the pushing of this that would lead to a great breakthrough in maths, but we will get to this a little later on. For now, just accept that some equations are limited in what the $x$ or $y$ values can be.

The equation of a circle

This equation represents a circle in 2D space:

$$x^2 + y^2 = 1$$

How?

Take any point in the space -- $(x,y)$.

How far is that point from the origin $(0,0)$?

Imagine a line extending from the point to the $x$-axis, and another from the point to the origin. It's a right angled triangle. We can find the distance of this point to the origin using Pythagoras' theorem.

$\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$

The square root of whatever number we set this to equal will denote the distance that all solutions must be from the origin.

Example:

$\sqrt{x^2+y^2} = 9 \to x^2 +y^2 = 3$

All solutions must be a distance of 3 away from the origin.

If we want a circle with a distance of only 1, then the square root of 1 is just 1, meaning this gives us the equation of the unit circle:

$$x^2+y^2=1$$

The constant on the right determines the range of what $x$ or $y$ can be. It is after all the length of the radius. It is the distance that the set of all points forming the circle must be equal to.

Every angle on the unit circle corresponds to a unique point on the circumference.
