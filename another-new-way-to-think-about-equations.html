Linear Algebra

Another new way to think about equations

$4x-2y=2$

We are inclined to think of the $x$ and $y$ as being only those values that make the equation true.

They are the pairs that make the equation true and are not the pairs that don't.

However, a better framing to approach Linear Algebra (LA) with is that all pairs of $(x,y)$ do exist.

Collectively, all these pairs describe a space.

So, that's the first thing: when we see an equation we check how many variables there are and imagine a space with that many dimensions/axes.

However, how should we conceptualize the coefficients?

$4x$ and $-2y$.

How do you multiple an axis?

You multiply every point on it.

Imagine a point $(3,4)$.

We move across the $x$-axis to (4 x 3) 12, and down the $y$-axis to (-2 x 4) -8.

Our new location is $(12, -8)$.

We can imagine this, not just as hopping over to this new point, but as dragging that old (3, 4) over to this new location, i.e. the space is changed.

We imagine that happens for every possible $(x,y)$ coordinate.

So, now when we see the equation $4x-2y=2$, we imagine:

  1. A space with two axes.
  2. Every coordinate being moved as specified by the coefficients.

However, notice that our equation involves the summing of $x$ and $y$ after they have been scaled by their coefficient.

$\large (12, -8)\to 12 -8 =\fbox{4}$

One value must exist in a one-dimensional space -- a line.

Our (12, -8) coordinate lives at 4 on a line.

We can imagine this happens to every coordinate in the scaled up space -- the 2D space collapses onto a 1D line.

But what about the constant?

We imagine the constant's value as a point on the new line (2).

We can not only see that our coordinate was not a solution, but also the distance it is from one, i.e. +2.

Finally, we could also imagine the reverse occurring:

From where the constant's value 2 is on the new line, we could imagine a 2D space appearing and all the possible coordinates whose signed sum $x-y$ is 2 shooting up and down from the 2.
