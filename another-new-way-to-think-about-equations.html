Linear Algebra

Another new way to think about equations

$4x-2y=2$

We are inclined to think of the $x$ and $y$ as being only those values that make the equation true. They are the pairs that make the equation true and are not the pairs that don't.

However, a better framing to approach Linear Algebra (LA) with is that all pairs of $(x,y)$ do exist. Collectively, all these pairs describe a space.

So, that's the first thing: when we see an equation we check how many variables there are and imagine a space with that many dimensions/axes.

However, how should we conceptualize the coefficients?

$4x$ and $-2y$.

How do you multiply an axis? You multiply every point on it.

Imagine a point $(3,4)$.

We move across the $x$-axis to (4 x 3) 12, and down the $y$-axis to (-2 x 4) -8.

Our new location is $(12, -8)$.

We can imagine this, not just as hopping over to this new point, but as dragging that old (3, 4) over to this new location, i.e. the space is changed.

We imagine that happens for every possible $(x,y)$ coordinate.

So, now when we see the equation $4x-2y=2$, we imagine:

  1. A space with two axes.
  2. Every coordinate being moved as specified by the coefficients.
What happens when the x-coefficient or y-coefficient increases or decreases?
Moving one unit along the horizontal or vertical direction corresponds to moving a greater or lesser distance relative to the original spacing. If $4x$ → moving 1 unit along the $x$-direction behaves like moving 4 units in terms of the original spacing. If $\frac{1}{2}y$ → moving 2 units along the $y$-direction behaves like moving 1 unit in terms of the original spacing. Larger coefficients stretch the space along that direction, while smaller coefficients compress it.
What happens when a coefficient becomes negative?
The direction reverses. For example, while positive $y$ normally moves upward, a negative $y$-coefficient causes positive movement in $y$ to move downward instead. The same applies horizontally for $x$. You can drag the sliders quickly between positive and negative values to make reflections easier to notice visually.
What happens if both coefficients scale by the same amount?
$x-y$ and $4x-4y$ Both produce the same overall geometric structure. However, the second scales all outputs by 4. So moving one unit in the transformed space of the second equation corresponds to moving four units in the transformed space of the first.
What happens when one coefficient becomes much larger than the other?
What matters most is not usually the absolute size of the coefficients, but their relationship to each other. Changing the balance between the coefficients changes which directions are stretched most, which directions dominate the transformation, and how the transformed space is oriented.
What happens when a coefficient becomes 0?
A coefficient of zero removes sensitivity to that direction. If the y-coefficient becomes 0, then moving up and down no longer changes the transformation.

However, notice that our equation involves the summing of $x$ and $y$ after they have been scaled by their coefficient.

$\large (12, -8)\to 12 -8 =\fbox{4}$

One value must exist in a one-dimensional space -- a line.

Our (12, -8) coordinate lives at 4 on a line.

We can imagine this happens to every coordinate in the scaled up space -- the 2D space collapses onto a 1D line.

But what about the constant?

We imagine the constant's value as a point on the new line (2).

We can not only see that our coordinate was not a solution, but also the distance it is from one, i.e. +2.

Finally, we could also imagine the reverse occurring:

From where the constant's value 2 is on the new line, we could imagine a 2D space appearing and all the possible coordinates whose signed sum $x-y$ is 2 shooting up and down from the 2.
