Complex Multiplication

You might be thinking:

Wait.

So we couldn't solve a problem.

We said, "Whatever the solution to this problem is, let's call it '$i$'."

Then we said, "Great. Now I know the solution! It's $i$."

And that is somewhat true.

However, the really strange thing was that this "solution" turned out to behave consistent with the rules of arithmetic and algebra.

Complex numbers can be added, subtracted, multiplied, and divided without anything breaking.

Then something even more interesting was observed. They introduced a new geometric meaning for multiplication.

Addition

Complex numbers follow the rules of algebra. For addition, the real and imaginary parts add independently. If we travel in one direction for some distance, and then travel in another direction, we can add the components of each part of our trip together to find the total distance we have traveled in each direction.

For multiplication, we can use the same algebraic methods.

Multiplication with $i$

Earlier, we saw that 1 multiplied by $i$ leads to a $90^\circ$ rotation up onto the imaginary axis (one unit high).

What do you think will happen with the following?

$4 \times i$

$4 \times i = 4i$

$3 \times (1+i)$

$3\times (1+i)=3+3i$

$(2+3i)(1+2i)$

Using ordinary algebra:

$(2+3i)(1+2i)$

$=2+4i+3i+6i^2$

$=2+7i-6$

$=-4+7i$.

$(2-4i)(1+2i)$

$(2-4i)(1+2i)$

$=2+4i-4i-8i^2$

$=2+8$

$=10$.

The Deeper Meaning

There is another way to think about complex multiplication. This interpretation was a massive game changer.

Try to discover it for yourself.

You will need to use the Pythagorean theorem and the arctangent to find angles (hint, hint).
