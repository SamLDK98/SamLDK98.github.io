Coordinates in Circular Motion

If we graphed the angle as a point moved around the circle, we would see a straight line. As time increases, the angle increases.

If we reset to $0^\circ$ after each revolution, we would see a repeating sawtooth pattern.

However, what if we tracked the coordinates linked to the angle during a revolution?

Let's take the $x$-coordinate, or as we will now call it, $\cos(\theta)$.

As the point travels around the circle, the $x$-coordinate moves from:

$$1 \rightarrow 0 \rightarrow -1 \rightarrow 0 \rightarrow 1$$

and then repeats.

At first glance this sounds similar to the sawtooth pattern. The value still moves back and forth between a maximum and minimum.

However, notice that while the angle increases at a constant rate, the coordinate does not.

  • Near the left and right edges of the circle, the point moves mostly up or down, so the $x$-coordinate changes only slowly.
  • Near the top or bottom of the circle, the point moves mostly left and right rather than up or down, so the $x$-coordinate changes much more rapidly.

Consequently, we see a smooth curve when graphed.

If we instead tracked $\sin(\theta)$, the $y$-coordinate, we would see the same pattern except the starting position would be shifted.

$$\cos(0^\circ)= 1$$

$$\sin(0^\circ) = 0$$

As a result, the sine oscillation starts at the midpoint of its cycle, whereas the cosine oscillation starts at its maximum.

A single point moving around a circle therefore generates two oscillations simultaneously: one horizontal and one vertical.

It might seem odd. The angle is changing at a constant rate, yet the coordinates trace out smooth wave patterns.

In other words, uniform motion around a circle produces oscillatory behaviour when viewed through its coordinates.

Oscillations vs Waves

An oscillation is a quantity that repeatedly varies between larger and smaller values.

The sine and cosine curves we have just derived are examples of oscillations.

However, these oscillations currently exist at a single location.

A wave emerges when an oscillation is distributed across space.
