2: Measuring Rotation (Degrees and Radians)

Angles as Differences between Directions

On a line, you can travel in two directions: left or right.

On a plane, you can travel in infinitely many directions.

A circle can be thought of as a set of all possible directions.

Angles are just a way of quantifying differences between directions.

Differences between Directions as Rotations in Orientation

Because we are talking about directions, it might be tempting to intuit some sense of distance or motion.

When see an angle depicted visually, it is typically a curved line. But notice that the length of this curved line is arbitrary.

It may therefore be a misleading visual aid.

It we imagine the circle partitioned as shown below, we can see more clearly what angle refers to. Notice that the curved lines all cross the same number of partitions (6), regardless of their length or even wonkiness.

Angle can be seen as a measure of how many of these lines must be crossed to rotate one orientation to the other.

We can think of each partition as a discrete orientation, and each turn to a new partition as a unit of rotation.

Ways to Measure Angles

Degrees

The Babylonians partitioned circles into 360 such parts — we call them degrees.

We will focus on multiplies of $30^\circ$ and $45^\circ$, because they work cleanly with equilateral triangles and squares, respectively.

Radians

While degrees arise from an arbitrary partitioning of the circle, radians emerge naturally from the relationship between a circle's circumference and its radius.

Imagine taking a line the length of a circle's radius.

Then you wrap it around the circle's circumference.

How many more would we need to make it all the way around?

The answer is $\approx 6.283$.

Which makes sense because the circumference of a circle $= 2\pi r$. In other words, $2\pi$ times the radius.

So for a unit circle we can take $2\pi$ as the measure of a full rotation around the circle.

Imagine marking off an arc whose length is exactly equal to the radius. The angle subtended by this arc is defined as 1 radian. Since the circumference of a unit circle is $2\pi$, $\approx 6.283$ such arcs fit around the circle. Therefore, one full rotation is $2\pi$ radians.

If one complete rotation around a circle is $2\pi$, then:

  • half a rotation is $\pi$,
  • a quarter rotation is $\frac{\pi}{2}$,
  • and so on.

It's worth getting familiar with both degrees and radians. I've created a game below for you to brute force this into memory.

Once you know $0^\circ, 90^\circ, 180^\circ, 270^\circ,$ and $360^\circ$, you can work out the other points based on adding or subtracting either $30^\circ$ or $45^\circ$.

The radii are trickier:

  • Multiples of $30^\circ$ are typically expressed in sixths of $\pi$.
  • Multiples of $45^\circ$ are typically expressed in fourths of $\pi$.
  • $90^\circ$ and $180^\circ$ are expressed as halves.

However, we can often express these fractions in reduced forms.
