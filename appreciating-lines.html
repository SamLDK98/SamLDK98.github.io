Linear Algebra

Appreciating lines

Straight lines are very interesting and I am not being remotely sarcastic.

$4x-2y=2$

A straight line captures this equation, and this equation is more complex than it seems.

Equations are statements: these two things are equal.

This one contains the idea of variables -- we don't yet know what is being declared to be equal...

We often talk about variables as unknowns. But perhaps they are better thought of as possibilities.

We define a category of thing, let's say, all the real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$), and we say $x$ and $y$ could be any one of these things.

We imagine all the possibilities of $x$ running along a horizontal axis, and all the possibilities of $y$ running along a vertical axis.

If we were not faced with any constraints (i.e. equations), we could have any pair of $(x,y)$.

We can imagine all of these points (the set of all possibilities) as a space, and being of two dimensions, that space is a 2D plane.

However, our equation limits this range of possibilities.

It says, sure, $x$ can be anything, but whatever that thing is, $y$ must then be the thing that makes our statement true.

This is a constraint on the possible ordered pairs of $(x,y)$.

The pairs of values that solve the constraint $4x-2y=2$ are the set of all possibilities for this specific constraint.

We can isolate our variables to more explicitly express this relationship, i.e. when $x$ is some value, what must $y$ be.

$4x-2y=2$ → $2x-y = 1$ → $\fbox{y = 2x -1}$

So, if:

  • $x = 1$, then $y=1$
  • $x=2$, then $y=3$
  • $x=3$, then $y=5$

The coordinates of our equation include: (1,1), (2, 3), (3,5), and so on.

We notice that as $x$ changes, $y$ changes at a constant rate -- always by +2.

We could draw and connect these points and they would form a straight line.

The line shows a constraint relating variables.

The line shows the set of all possible solutions to our equation.
