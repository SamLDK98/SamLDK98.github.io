Linear Algebra

Appreciating lines

Straight lines are very interesting and I am not being remotely sarcastic. Unfortunately, we only have time to discuss one reason why.

$4x-2y=2$

A straight line perfectly captures this equation, and this equation is more complex than it seems.

Equations are statements: these two things are equal.

This equation contains the idea of variables: we do not yet know what is being declared to be equal.

We often talk about variables as unknowns. But perhaps they are better thought of as possibilities.

We define a category of thing, let's say, all the real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$), and we say $x$ and $y$ could be any one of these things.

We imagine all the possibilities of $x$ running along a horizontal axis, and all the possibilities of $y$ running along a vertical axis.

Now, without any constraints, we could have any point $(x,y)$, and we can imagine all of these points, the set of all possibilities, as a space. Being of two dimensions, that space is a 2D plane.

However, our equation limits this range of possibilities. It says, sure, $x$ can be anything, but whatever that thing is, $y$ must be the exact thing that makes our statement true.

This is a constraint on the possible ordered pairs of $(x,y)$.

The pairs of values that solve the constraint $4x-2y=2$ are the set of all possibilities for this specific constraint.

We can isolate our variables to more explicitly express this relationship, i.e. when $x$ is some value what must $y$ be.

$x=\frac{2 +2y}{4}$

$y=\frac{2-4x}{-2}$

When $x$ is 1, $y$ must be $\frac{2 -4(1)}{-2}=1$.

When $x$ is 2, $y$ must be $\frac{2 -4(2)}{-2}=3$.

When $x$ is 3, $y$ must be $\frac{2 -4(3)}{-2}=5$.

The limited coordinates of our equation include: $(1,1)$, $(2,3)$, $(3,5)$, and so on.

We notice that as $x$ changes, $y$ changes at a constant rate: always by +2.

We could draw and connect these points, with respect to our 2D space. These points are the set of all possible solutions to our equation, and they would form a straight line.

These lines can model much real-life activity. Think of anything where as one thing increases or decreases, so does another thing, proportionally.

The line is not merely a shape. It is the geometric manifestation of a constraint relating variables.
