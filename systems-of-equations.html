Linear Algebra

Systems of equations

If we have two different lines, unless they are parallel, they must intersect at some point.

$\large \begin{cases} 2x - y = 1\\3x + 3y = 15 \end{cases}$

The intersection is the only $(x,y)$ pair that will satisfy BOTH equations.

There's two things worth noticing here.

Firstly...

The number of variables in the equations determines the dimensionality of the space we are in. Here we have two variables, $x$ and $y$, so we are working in a two-dimensional coordinate system.

Secondly...

Each equation acts as a constraint on that space. Geometrically, linear equations carve lower-dimensional surfaces out of higher-dimensional spaces.

  • One equation in two variables:
    • defines a line in 2D space.
  • Two equations in two variables:
    • two lines in 2D space.
    • solution: typically a point.
  • One equation in three variables:
    • defines a plane in 3D space.
  • Two equations in three variables:
    • two planes in 3D space.
    • solution: typically a line.
  • Three equations in three variables:
    • three planes in 3D space.
    • solution: typically a point.

Each additional independent equation reduces the remaining degrees of freedom.

In this sense, solving simultaneous equations is the process of progressively shrinking the space of possible solutions.
