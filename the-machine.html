The Machine

Imagine:

$$\begin{cases} 4x - 2y = 2\\3x + 5y = 7 \end{cases}$$

Let's temporarily forget the specific constants and replace them with variables.

$$\begin{cases} 4x - 2y = c_{1}\\3x + 5y = c_{2} \end{cases}$$

Imagine all the possible values of $c_{1}$ as an axis.

Do the same for $c_{2}$.

Put them together and we have a space of all possible values for $(c_{1}, c_{2})$.

This might seem a little odd. Typically we would be thinking about all the possible inputs in terms of a space, i.e. with $x$ and $y$ as our axes; whereas, now we are thinking about all the possible outputs.

Next, let's plot our actual values, 2 and 7, in this space.

Let's call this "output space".

Now, let's imagine all our possible values for $x$ and $y$ to create a similar "input space".

Let's guess an input that might solve the system, e.g. $x = 3$, $y = 1$.

Plot it in input space.

However, we know that our inputs are weighted by coefficients.

Currently this coordinate represents the expression $x - y$, not $4x - 2y$.

Our coefficients tell us that...

  • For a one unit increase in the $x$ direction, we move 4 units along the $c_{1}$ axis and 3 units along the $c_{2}$ axis.
  • For a one unit increase in the $y$ direction, we move -2 units along the $c_{1}$ axis and 5 units along the $c_{2}$ axis.

So, our guess $(3, 1)$ would need to move:

  • 12 units along the $c_{1}$ axis and 9 units along the $c_{2}$ axis.
  • -2 units along the $c_{1}$ axis and 5 units along the $c_{2}$ axis.

This would leave us at the coordinate $(10, 14)$.

$(10, 14)$ is not $(2, 7)$ -- not a solution!
