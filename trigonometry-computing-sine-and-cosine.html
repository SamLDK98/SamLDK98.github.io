Interpreting Sine and Cosine

Opposite and Adjacent Sides

As we saw in the last chapter, there are no 'opposite' and 'adjacent' sides intrinsic to the triangle. They are relative to specific angles.

The opposite side of an angle is the side connecting the endpoints of the two sides that form the angle.

The bigger an angle is, the longer its opposite must be.

For right-angled triangles, the 90 degree angle will always have the largest opposite side; however, of the remaining two (unless they are equal), there will be a larger and smaller angle.

The larger angle has the longer opposite side. The smaller angle has the shorter opposite side.

So:

  • The shorter side appears in either the sine of the smaller angle or the cosine of the larger angle.
  • The longer side appears in either the cosine of the smaller angle or the sine of the larger angle.

Thinking of Sine and Cosine as Percentages

A ratio compares the size of two quantities.

One way to think about a ratio is as describing how many times one quantity fits into another. For example:

$$\frac{8}{4}=2$$

means that 4 fits into 8 exactly twice. However, when a ratio is less than 1, it is often easier to think of it as a percentage. If one quantity 'fits into' another 0.6 times, it is equivalent to saying that it is $60\%$ of the size.

This is always the case for sine and cosine because the numerator is always a side shorter than the length of the hypotenuse.

$$\sin(\theta)=\frac{o}{h} \qquad \cos(\theta)=\frac{a}{h}$$

So if:

$$\sin(30^\circ)=\frac{1}{2}=0.5$$

then the opposite side is 50% the length of the hypotenuse.

Also, remember that the side opposite one acute angle is adjacent to the other. The side opposite the $30^\circ$ angle is also the side adjacent to the $60^\circ$ angle.

Therefore if $\sin(30^\circ)=50\%$, then:

$$\cos(60^\circ)=50\%$$

because they are referring to the same side of the right-angled triangle.

You can think of the sine of an angle as describing what fraction of the hypotenuse lies opposite that angle, while the cosine describes what fraction of the hypotenuse lies adjacent to it.

Thinking of Sine and Cosine in Terms of $x$- and $y$-Coordinates

Imagine a line of length 1 extending from the origin.

If the line points directly upward, its endpoint has coordinates $(0,1)$, so its height is 1.

As the line rotates, and becomes slanted, its length remains the same, but its height changes.

Sine tells us what fraction of the line's maximum possible height remains.

For example, if:

$$\sin(\theta)=0.5$$

then the height of the endpoint is half of what it would be if the line were perfectly vertical.

Similarly, cosine tells us what fraction of the line's maximum possible horizontal length remains.

If:

$$\cos(\theta)=0.75$$

then the horizontal position of the endpoint is 75% of what it would be if the line were perfectly horizontal.
