Trigonometry

How to Think About Sine and Cosine

In the previous chapter we derived our first angle-ratio correspondences and introduced the names sine and cosine.

In this chapter we will formalise some of the terminology and develop a more intuitive understanding of these relationships.

Hypotenuse, Opposite, and Adjacent

The opposite side of an angle is the side connecting the endpoints of the two sides that form the angle.

The larger the angle between two line segments, the longer the line connecting their endpoints must be.

In a right-angled triangle, no angle can be greater than $90^\circ$, so the line connecting the endpoints of the right angle must be the longest side.

We call the side opposite the right angle the hypotenuse.

It is useful to be able to spot it quickly. One way I like to identify it is to imagine completing the triangle into a rectangle or square. The hypotenuse is the side that cuts across the interior -- the diagonal line.

If there is another angle that is close to $90^\circ$ in the triangle, then it can be difficult to spot the hypotenuse. There will be two long sides. Such examples feature in 'Expert Mode' in the mini-game below.

There is always a fixed hypotenuse, because there is always a $90^\circ$ angle in a right triangle; however, there is no fixed 'opposite' or 'adjacent' side.

They are literally just descriptions of sides in relation to an angle that you pick.

"The side opposite to this angle."

"The side adjacent to this angle."

'Adjacent' here really means, "The side forming this angle that is not the hypotenuse."

Notice that the side opposite one angle is the side adjacent to the other!

Unless the other angles are both the same ($45^\circ$), there must be a longer and a shorter side.

The larger angle has the longer opposite side.

The smaller angle has the shorter opposite side.

So:

  • The shorter side appears in either the sine of the smaller angle or the cosine of the larger angle.
  • The longer side appears in either the cosine of the smaller angle or the sine of the larger angle.

Right Triangle Shapes

While the $90^\circ$ angle is fixed, the other angles are free to co-vary (they must add to $90^\circ$), meaning the triangle can take on different shapes.

Sine and Cosine

We saw that sine and cosine were useful ratios for working out the lengths of unknown 'sides'.

Sine and cosine take an angle as input and return a number. We can therefore think of them as functions. The symbol $\theta$ ("theta") is conventionally used to represent an unknown angle.

$$\sin(\theta)=\frac{o}{h} \qquad \cos(\theta)=\frac{a}{h}$$

In a right-angled triangle, the hypotenuse is always the longest side. The other two sides must therefore be some fraction of its length.

$$0<\frac{\text{Either other side}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}<1$$

Sine and cosine are therefore answering the question, "What fraction of the hypotenuse is this side?"

Fractions, or numbers less than 1, are often easier to think of as percentages. If one quantity 'fits into' another 0.6 times, it is equivalent to saying that it is $60\%$ of the size.

For example, if sine of $30^\circ$ is $.5$, then the side opposite this angle is 50% the length of the hypotenuse.

$$\sin(30^\circ)=\frac{1}{2}=0.5=50 \%$$

Also, remember that the side opposite one acute angle is adjacent to the other.

The side opposite the $30^\circ$ angle is the same side that is adjacent to the $60^\circ$ angle.

Therefore:

$$\sin(30^\circ) = 50\%$$

implies

$$\cos(60^\circ) = 50\%$$

because both ratios refer to the same side of the triangle.

You can think of the sine of an angle as describing what fraction of the hypotenuse lies opposite that angle, while the cosine describes what fraction of the hypotenuse lies adjacent to it.
