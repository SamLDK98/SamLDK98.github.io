Sine and Cosine

Opposite and Adjacent Sides

As we saw in the last chapter, there are no 'opposite' and 'adjacent' sides intrinsic to the triangle. They are relative to specific angles.

The opposite side of an angle is the side connecting the endpoints of the two sides that form the angle.

The bigger an angle is, the longer its opposite must be.

For right-angled triangles, the 90 degree angle will always have the largest opposite side; however, of the remaining two (unless equal), there will be a bigger and smaller angle.

The larger angle has the longer opposite side. The smaller angle has the shorter opposite side.

So:

  • The shorter side refers to either the sine of the smaller angle or the cosine of the larger angle.
  • The longer side refers to either the cosine of the smaller angle or the sine of the larger angle.

You can think of the sine of an angle as describing what fraction of the hypotenuse lies opposite that angle, while the cosine describes what fraction of the hypotenuse lies adjacent to it.

Thinking of Sine and Cosine as Percentages

$\sin(30)=0.5$ is the same as saying $\sin(30)=50\%$

But 50% makes it far more obvious that it's a comparative ratio.

If the sine of an angle is $50\%$, this means that its opposite side is $50\%$ the length of the hypotenuse.

Likewise if cosine is $75\%$, the adjacent side is $75\%$ of the hypotenuse.
