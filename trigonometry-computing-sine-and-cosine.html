Sine and Cosine

Sine and cosine were originally not thought of as 'functions' per se.

They were just names for specific ratios between sides in right-angled triangles.

$$\large \text{sine} = \frac{o}{h} \qquad \large \text{cosine} = \frac{a}{h}$$

In the previous example, we saw that sine could be $\frac{1}{2}$ or $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$, depending on which angle we were considering.

So, although they are 'ratios between sides', it's important to thing of them as associated with angles, not the sides themselves.

You can think of the sine of an angle as describing what fraction of the hypotenuse lies opposite that angle, while the cosine describes what fraction of the hypotenuse lies adjacent to it.

Historically, deriving sine and cosine values often required lengthy geometric arguments. As a result, mathematicians compiled trigonometric tables so that these values could be looked up rather than repeatedly derived. Today, calculators compute these values almost instantly using numerical algorithms based on infinite power series.
