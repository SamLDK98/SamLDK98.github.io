Trigonometry

Right-Angled Triangles

Previously we saw that the lengths of a triangle's sides meant it must have a certain shape.

A triangle with sides 5, 7, and 10 must have angles $27.7^\circ$, $40.5^\circ$, and $111.8^\circ$.

However, we also saw that angles only determine the necessary ratios between side lengths, not the absolute lengths.

A triangle with angles $27.7^\circ$, $40.5^\circ$, and $111.8^\circ$ could have sides $5, 7, 10$ or $50, 70, 100$ or any proportional scaling of these numbers.

However, in the early years of trigonometry, while this deterministic relationship between angles and side ratios was known to exist, there was no general procedure for calculating specific instances of it.

Instead, a variety of geometric methods were used to derive individual cases, and the results were gradually recorded in books called Trigonometric Tables for future use.

Right-angled triangles became the focus because they were practical for most use-cases and greatly simplified the process of working out the relationships, since we start off already knowing one angle.

In this chapter we are going to get familiar with right-angled triangles and use some geometry to work out the mapping between certain angles and certain side ratios.

Hypotenuse

If you think about it, the larger the angle between two line segments, the longer the line connecting their endpoints must be.

In a right-angled triangle, no angle can be greater than $90^\circ$, so the line connecting the endpoints of the right angle must be the longest side.

We call the side opposite the right angle the hypotenuse.

It is useful to be able to spot it quickly. One way I like to identify it is to imagine completing the triangle into a rectangle or square. The hypotenuse is the side that cuts across the interior.

Practice identifying the hypotenuse until it feels natural.

The Other Sides

Unless the other angles are both the same ($45^\circ$), there must be a longer and a shorter leg.

However, if the difference between them is great (e.g. if one angle were $85^\circ$), then the longer leg can become indistinguishable from the hypotenuse by eye.

Later, when we begin thinking of the other legs as either 'opposite' or 'adjacent', this will always be in relation to whichever angle we have chosen to work with.

Really, there is no fixed 'opposite' or 'adjacent' leg in a right-angled triangle in the same way that there is a fixed hypotenuse.

Finding Ratios Geometrically

While the $90^\circ$ angle is fixed, the other angles are free to co-vary, meaning the triangle can take on different shapes.

As the $90^\circ$ angle determines that its opposite side, the hypotenuse, will be the longest, the other sides can only ever be some fraction of it.

$$0<\frac{\text{Either other side}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}<1$$

We know that certain sets of angles result in certain ratios between the sides, so let's try to work out what these ratios must be for some right-angled triangles.

Consider a triangle with angles $90^\circ$, $60^\circ$, and $30^\circ$.

What are the proportions between its sides?

By eye, the shorter leg appears to be roughly half or a third of the hypotenuse, while the longer leg looks to be perhaps four-fifths of its length.

But how can we determine these proportions precisely?

To simplify the calculations, let the hypotenuse have length $1$.

Imagine a carbon copy of this right-angled triangle. If we flipped it over we could place the two together.

The two triangles now form an equilateral triangle.

The two $30^\circ$ angles combine to form a $60^\circ$ angle, while the other two angles are already $60^\circ$. The larger triangle therefore has three $60^\circ$ angles.

Given that all the angles are the same, the side lengths must also be the same. Therefore, the bottom side has length 1.

Since the two triangles are identical, each contributes the same length to the bottom side. Therefore each half must be $\large \frac{1}{2}$.

Returning to our original triangle, we now have:

  • $\text{hypotenuse} = 1$
  • $\text{shorter side} = \large \frac{1}{2}$

We can find the remaining side using Pythagoras' theorem:

$$ \begin{aligned} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 + x^2 &= 1^2 \\ \frac{1}{4} + x^2 &= 1 \\ x^2 &= \frac{3}{4} \\ x &= \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \end{aligned} $$

We now have:

  • $\text{hypotenuse} = 1$
  • $\text{shorter side} = \large \frac{1}{2}$
  • $\text{longer side} = \large \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$

So, using only geometry and taking advantage of the symmetry of this shape, we have determined the proportions of the sides relative to the hypotenuse.

The final step is to work out what the ratios must be from the perspective of each angle.

It may be worth remembering that the greater the angle between two line segments, the longer the line connecting their endpoints must be. Consequently, if we choose the larger angle, the opposite side will be the longer leg.

Let's work with both angles to see how this changes the labels.

So, from the perspective of the $30^\circ$ angle:

The opposite = $\frac{1}{2}$ (short leg)

The adjacent = $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ (long leg)

  • The ratio between the opposite and hypotenuse is: $\frac{o}{h} = \frac{0.5}{1} = 0.5$
  • The ratio between the adjacent and hypotenuse is: $\frac{a}{h}= \frac{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}{1} =\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$

And from the perspective of the $60^\circ$ angle:

The opposite = $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ (long leg)

The adjacent = $\frac{1}{2}$ (short leg)

  • The ratio between the opposite and hypotenuse is: $\frac{o}{h}= \frac{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}{1} =\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
  • The ratio between the adjacent and hypotenuse is: $\frac{a}{h}= \frac{0.5}{1} = 0.5$

Notice that both perspectives describe the same triangle.

In each case we discover the same three side length ratios:

$$1:\frac{1}{2}: \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$$

How do we know which angle belongs with which side?

Recall that larger angles must be opposite longer sides.

Since

$$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} > \frac{1}{2}$$

the larger angle, $60^\circ$, must be opposite the side of length $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

Consequently, the $60^\circ$ angle must lie between the sides of lengths $1$ and $\frac{1}{2}$.

Likewise, the $30^\circ$ angle must be opposite the side of length $\frac{1}{2}$, and therefore lie between the sides of lengths $1$ and $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

Labelling sides in a right-angled triangle as opposite and adjacent became such a common practice that these ratios were given their own names:

$$\large \text{sine} = \frac{o}{h} \qquad \large \text{cosine} = \frac{a}{h}$$

Mathematicians would then record the values of these ratios for many different angles in books called Trigonometric Tables.

A table entry might have looked something like this:

Angle Sine Cosine
$30^\circ$ $\frac{1}{2}$ $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
$60^\circ$ $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ $\frac{1}{2}$

While the above example was relatively straightforward to derive geometrically, many other angles were considerably more difficult. Trigonometric tables therefore allowed people to look up these values directly rather than deriving them from scratch each time.
