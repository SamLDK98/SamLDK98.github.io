Trigonometry

Right-Angled Triangles

Previously we saw that the lengths of a triangle's sides meant it must have a certain shape.

A triangle with sides 5, 7, and 10 must have angles $27.7^\circ$, $40.5^\circ$, and $111.8^\circ$.

However, we also saw that angles only determine the necessary ratios between side lengths, not the absolute lengths.

A triangle with angles $27.7^\circ$, $40.5^\circ$, and $111.8^\circ$ could have sides $5, 7, 10$ or $50, 70, 100$ or any proportional scaling of these numbers.

However, while this deterministic relationship between angles and side ratios was known to exist, there was no general procedure for calculating specific instances of it.

Instead, a variety of geometric methods were used to derive individual cases, and the results were gradually recorded in books called Trigonometric Tables for future use.

Right-angled triangles became the focus because they were practical for most use-cases and greatly simplified the process of working out the relationships, since we start off already knowing one angle.

In this chapter we are going to get familiar with right-angled triangles are use some geometry to work out the mapping between certain angles and certain side ratios.

Hypotenuse

A right-angled triangle has one angle at $90^\circ$. The longest side, the hypotenuse, cannot connect to this corner.

No matter how a right-angled triangle is rotated, the hypotenuse remains the side opposite the $90^\circ$ angle.

One way I like to visualise the hypotenuse is to imagine completing the triangle into a rectangle or square. The hypotenuse is the side that cuts across the interior.

Finding Ratios Geometrically

While the $90^\circ$ angle is fixed, the other angles are free to co-vary, meaning we can have different shapes.

The $90^\circ$ angle determines that its opposite side will be the longest. That means the ratio between it and the other two will always be less than 1.

We know that certain sets of angles will result in certain ratios between the sides, so let's try to work out what this ratio is for same right-angled triangles.

Consider a triangle with angles $90^\circ$, $60^\circ$, and $30^\circ$.

What are the proportions between its sides?

To simplify the calculations, let the hypotenuse have length $1$.

Now duplicate the triangle and place the copy beside it.

The two triangles now form an equilateral triangle.

We can add the angles to see that all the angles of the equilateral triangle are $60^\circ$.

Given that all the angles are the same, all the side lengths must be the same. This means the side at the bottom must also be length $1$.

We can therefore tell that the size of the bottom of our original right-angled triangle must be $\frac{1}{2}$.

Because the resulting triangle is equilateral:

  • All three sides have length $1$.
  • All three angles are $60^\circ$.

The side that was split by our construction therefore has total length $1$, meaning each half has length $\frac{1}{2}$.

Returning to our original right triangle, we now know:

  • $\text{hypotenuse} = 1$
  • $\text{shorter side} = \frac{1}{2}$

We can then find the remaining side using Pythagoras:

$$\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 + x^2 = 1^2$$

$$\frac{1}{4} + x^2 = 1$$

$$x^2 = \frac{3}{4}$$

$$x = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$$

We now know:

  • $\text{hypotenuse} = 1$
  • $\text{leg 1} = \frac{1}{2}$
  • $\text{leg 2} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$

So, using just geometry and taking advantage of the symmetry of this shape, we have worked out what the side lengths must be relative to the hypotenuse.

The final step is work out what the ratios must be from the perspective of each angle.

We select the angle we want to find and label the sides accordingly.

So, from the perspective of the $30^\circ$ angle:

  • The ratio between the opposite and hypotenuse is: $\frac{o}{h} = \frac{0.5}{1} = 0.5$
  • The ratio between the adjacent and hypotenuse is: $\frac{a}{h}= \frac{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}{1} =\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$

And from the perspective of the $60^\circ$ angle:

  • The ratio between the opposite and adjacent is: $\frac{o}{h}= \frac{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}{1} =\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
  • The ratio between the adjacent and hypotenuse is: $\frac{a}{h}= \frac{0.5}{1} = 0.5$

These particular ratios were very common and so they were given names: sine and cosine.

$\large sine = \frac{o}{h}$

$\large cosine = \frac{a}{h}$

So we have worked out that for a right-angled triangle with an angle of either $60^\circ$ or $30^\circ$ (because if we have one, we must have the other), there must be one side that is half as big as the hypotenuse and another side that is $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ times as big as the hypotenuse.
