Linear Algebra

Determinants

The historical development of determinants is outlined in these sections on Leibniz and Cramer; however, they are not necessary to understand the contemporary geometric understanding of determinants, so feel free to skip ahead.

Leibniz's and Cramer's observations

To eliminate a variable in a system we need the coefficients to match across equations.

This can be ensured by multiplying the coefficients of the targeted variable by one another, e.g. $3 \cdot 5 = 5 \cdot 3$.

$$ \begin{cases} 2x + 3y = 7 \\ 4x + 5y = 1 \end{cases} \ \ \ \ \ \rightarrow \ \ \ \ \ \begin{cases} 5(2x + 3y = 7) \\ 3(4x + 5y = 1) \end{cases} \ \ \ \ \ \rightarrow \ \ \ \ \ \begin{cases} 10x + \boxed{15y} = 35 \\ 12x + \boxed{15y} = 3 \end{cases} $$

Algebraically:

$$\begin{cases} ax+by=e\\ cx+dy=f \end{cases} \ \ \ \ \to \ \ \ \ \begin{cases} d(ax+by=e)\\ b(cx+dy=f) \end{cases} \ \ \ \ \to \ \ \ \ \begin{cases} adx+bdy=ed\\ bcx+bdy=bf \end{cases}$$

To eliminate the variable, subtract:

$$(adx+bdy)-(bcx+bdy)=ed-bf$$

We are left with:

$$(adx)-(bcx)=ed-bf$$

The $x$ can be factored out:

$$x(ad-bc)=ed-bf$$

And isolated:

$$x=\frac{ed-bf}{ad-bc}$$

We could do the same for $y$ and we would be left with:

$$y = \frac{af - ec}{ad - bc}$$

Leibniz observation

  • Regardless of the particular outputs of an equation, the coefficients can tell you if the system is uniquely solvable. That is, if $ad-bc=0$, elimination removes not only the target variable, but also the remaining variable coefficient, preventing the unique isolation of a variable.

This allowed mathematicians to quickly identify when a system could not be uniquely solved.

Cramer's observation

  • The isolated-$x$ numerator replaces the $x$-coefficients ($a,c$) with the outputs $(e, f)$.
  • While the isolated-$y$ numerator replaces the $y$-coefficients $(b,d)$ with the outputs $(e,f)$.

Again, this helped speed up the process of solving systems, and revealed a little more structure.

Geometric interpretation of determinants

Determinants were originally viewed as clever algebraic tools for quickly determining whether a system could be uniquely solved.

But in the 19th century, mathematicians realized that determinants also describe geometry: they equal the signed area scaling factor of a transformed unit square. That is, the factor by which the area increased or decreased, and whether the orientation was flipped, as indicated by the positive or negative sign.

The unit square is simply the square formed by the standard basis vectors:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Now consider the transformation:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$$

The transformation moves the basis vectors to new coordinates:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} a \\ c \end{bmatrix} \qquad \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} b \\ d \end{bmatrix}$$

The unit square becomes a with one pair of sides formed by $\begin{bmatrix} a \\ c \end{bmatrix}$ and the other pair by $\begin{bmatrix} b \\ d \end{bmatrix}$.

A parallelogram has four sides whose opposite sides are parallel, i.e. a square, rectangle, or more general slanted forms.

By calculating $ad-bc$ we can find this 'signed area scaling factor'.

Example

Imagine:

$$ A= \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2\\ 3 & 5 \end{bmatrix} $$

$$ \begin{vmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = ad-bc $$

$$ \det(A) = (4)(5)-(-2)(3) = 20+6 = 26 $$

This means the transformation scales area by a factor of $26$.

Note that the determinant is not the area itself; it is the factor by which area scales relative to the original unit square.

Types of determinants

Take:

$$ \det(A)= \begin{vmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{vmatrix} $$

* Vertical bars denote a determinant operation.

Positive determinants mean the area is scaled by that factor and orientation is preserved.

Negative determinants mean the area is scaled by that factor and the orientation is reversed.

Determinants with magnitude < 1 (fractions or irrationals) mean the area is reduced by that factor.

Determinants that equal zero mean that the area is reduced to nothing, indicating the transformation has collapsed the plane to a lower-dimensional shape (a line or a point). The transformed basis vectors must be linearly dependent.

When $\det(A)=0$ -> the transformation is not invertible. Different input vectors now map to the same output vector, meaning the transformation can no longer be uniquely reversed.

Extra intuition

The subtraction in $ad-bc$ measures the net independent area generated by the transformed basis vectors. When the vectors begin pointing in the same direction, the two terms cancel and the area collapses toward zero.
