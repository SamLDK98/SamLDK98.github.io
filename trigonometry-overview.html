Why Triangles?

Imagine an angle between two lines of fixed length: 3 (red) and 6 (blue).

The angle could be anything between $0^\circ$ and $180^\circ$. It's completely free to vary.

Practice swinging the little red line around and around until you understand this deeply. You can then practice with the big blue line just for fun.

However, if there were another line of fixed length connecting their endpoints, that angle becomes fixed.

Think about it like this: you can only connect three lines of fixed length to form a triangle in one specific way.

For example, if we had three lines with lengths 3, 6, and 8, we could only make them form a triangle with the angles: $18.6^\circ$, $39.6^\circ$, and $121.8^\circ$.

Go ahead and try to make a triangle with anything other than these angles, given these lengths.

And once you have failed at that, you should arrive at the first key realisation: side lengths determine the angle sizes in triangles.

Ah. But wait...

Is the same true in reverse?

With angles fixed at $18.6^\circ$, $39.6^\circ$, and $121.8^\circ$, can we form a triangle with side lengths other than 3, 6, and 8?

Yes — provided the side lengths are scaled proportionally.

The key realisation here is that the ratios between the sides are being preserved, i.e. it is the ratios between sides that determine angles!

So just to hammer those points home once again:

A specific set of side lengths determines a specific set of angles.

A specific set of angles determines specific ratios between side lengths.

Now, why are these properties so useful?

In the beginning it was simple: the dependencies that the extra constraint enforced meant astronomers, navigators, and other nerds of antiquity could infer angles and distances that were not directly observable.

If the length of one side of a triangle was known, information about the remaining sides and angles could often be deduced.

However, the significance of these relationships would ultimately extend far beyond practical geometry.

Notice what has happened: we began with an angle, a quantity describing rotation, and embedded it within a triangle.

This created a bridge between rotations and ratios of lengths.

Much of trigonometry can be understood as the study of this bridge.

In the chapters that follow, we will see that this connection links circular and linear ways of representing quantity, and allows us to work with oscillations and complex numbers.
