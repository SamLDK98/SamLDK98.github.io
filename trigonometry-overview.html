Trigonometry

Overview

"Trigonometry" translates to triangle measurement.

Yet angles are the real subject of interest; constructing triangles is just an effective method for working with them.

Angles can be understood as proportions of rotation around a circle; this interpretation eventually connects trigonometry to oscillations, complex numbers, and Euler's formula.

Typically, I like to move through these explanations conceptually, often with a focus on the historical development of ideas.

In this case, however, I think it's better to simply brute force some of the behaviour, structure, and relations into your head first.

Why do we need triangles to find angles?

An angle is a relational construct.

If you try to picture an angle, you need at least two independent things for the angle to exist between.

Let's say I am thinking of two intersecting lines — an angle exists between them.

There's no way for you to know how big or small my angle is without more information.

I could tell you the length of the lines:

  • line $a = 4$ (blue)
  • line $b = 5$ (yellow)

Still, the angle could be anything between $0^\circ$ and $180^\circ$.

To know what my angle is, you need to know the length of a line connecting these two.

This is because three lines that connect at their endpoints (a triangle) have certain properties that make inferring the angles of their corners possible with information about the line lengths.

In other words, three lines can be connected to form a triangle in only one way.

Knowing three sides ($a = 4$, $b = 5$, and $c = 6$) means we could only have a triangle with specific angles: $41.4^\circ$, $55.8^\circ$, and $82.8^\circ$.

Try yourself to form a triangle with these side lengths that does not have those angles. You should realise that angles determine the shape of a triangle. To change an angle would change the length of two sides.

Something might seem confusing.

You have just learned that specific triangles have specific angles.

However, I am only thinking of one angle between two lines. You could "draw" your third side anywhere that connects the two.

So does this not create different sets of angles depending on where you choose?

Actually, what we see is that the angles remain invariant.

However, notice that we are increasing every side proportionately.

So while a triangle with side lengths 4-5-6 determines the angles $41.4^\circ-55.8^\circ-82.8^\circ$.

The angles $41.4^\circ-55.8^\circ-82.8^\circ$ determine proportions, not absolute lengths. We could have side lengths 8-10-12, 12-15-18, etc.

The key insight is that knowing the proportions between sides tells us what the angles must be.

Right-angled Triangles

So, given the lengths of three sides of a triangle, we could work out the angles based on the ratios between them. However, the most common triangles we work with are right-angled triangles, which have an easier calculation process. Let's start there.

We can label the sides of right-angled triangles. The hypotenuse is fixed; however, the opposite and adjacent sides depend on which corner we are interested in the angle from.

From the three sides of a right-angled triangle, we can construct six named ratios: sine, cosine, tangent, cosecant, secant, and cotangent.

$\sin(\theta) =$ oh $\cos(\theta) =$ ah $\tan(\theta) =$ oa $\csc(\theta) =$ ho $\sec(\theta) =$ ha $\cot(\theta) =$ ao

We can find the mystery angle using whichever ratio is most convenient.

If all we know is that we have a right-angled triangle, we know that one angle is fixed to $90^\circ$; however, the other two are free to vary as the side lengths change, although they cannot vary independently. You can see below that as you extend either sides, both angles change.

If we fix a second angle, the third is fixed as well, since the angles of a triangle must sum to $180^\circ$.

We can still vary the side lengths proportionally and preserve the ratios.

You can experiment with changing this second angle and notice how the ratios change. Then fix the angle and scale the triangle; notice that the ratios remain unchanged.

This means that particular ratios are uniquely associated with particular sets of angles.

As the ratios depend on the angle, we can think of them as functions; for example:

$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{o}{h}$$

Such functions assume right-angled triangles. We give them an angle and receive a ratio between specific side lengths.

Degrees

Let's now think of angles as measuring rotation about a point.

We rotate a line counterclockwise about the center of the circle, beginning with the line pointing to the right along the $x$-axis at $0^\circ$.

Imagine a point where the line crosses the circumference of the circle. As we rotate the line, the point moves around the circumference.

When I say 'about', I am thinking of pinching the line at one end and swivelling it such that, at the other end, the point moves 'around' the circumference of the circle.

We have arbitrarily partitioned the circle into 360 parts, which we will call degrees. We will focus on multiples of $30^\circ$ and $45^\circ$, because they work cleanly with equilateral triangles and squares, respectively.

Also, take note of how coordinates are signed depending on quadrant:

$$ \begin{array}{c|c} \text{Quadrant} & \text{Coordinates } (x,y) \\ \hline \text{I} & (+, +) \\ \text{II} & (-, +) \\ \text{III} & (-, -) \\ \text{IV} & (+, -) \end{array} $$

Radians

While degrees arise from an arbitrary partitioning of the circle, radians are intrinsic to the geometry of circles.

Imagine taking the radius of a circle and wrapping it around the circumference, beginning at the $x$-axis (see widget below).

Now connect a line (ray) from the origin to the endpoint of this wrapped radius.

The angle created is defined as 1 radian, which is approximately equivalent to $57.3^\circ$.

How many radii would we need to complete one full rotation around the circle?

The answer is $2\pi$.

Why?

Circumference = $\pi \cdot \text{diameter}$

Diameter = $2r$

Circumference = $2\pi r$

If we have a unit circle, where $r = 1$, then the circumference is just $2\pi$, i.e. $2 \cdot 3.1415 = 6.283$.

If one complete rotation around a circle is $2\pi$ radians, then:

  • half a rotation is $\pi$ radians
  • a quarter rotation is $\frac{\pi}{2}$ radians
  • and so on.

It's worth getting familiar with these angles, in terms of both degrees and radii. I've created a game below for you to brute force this into memory.

Once you know $0^\circ, 90^\circ, 180^\circ, 270^\circ,$ and $360^\circ$, you can work out the other points based on adding or subtracting either $30^\circ$ or $45^\circ$.

The radii are trickier...

Multiples of $30^\circ$ are typically expressed in sixths of $\pi$. Multiples of $45^\circ$ are typically expressed in fourths of $\pi$.

Because:

$$30^\circ = \frac{\pi}{6} \qquad\text{and}\qquad 45^\circ = \frac{\pi}{4}$$

A full circle is $360^\circ = 2\pi$ radians. Therefore:

  • Dividing the circle into $12$ equal parts gives $30^\circ$ steps:

$$\frac{2\pi}{12} = \frac{\pi}{6} \text{ radians}$$

  • Dividing the circle into $8$ equal parts gives $45^\circ$ steps:

$$\frac{2\pi}{8} = \frac{\pi}{4} \text{ radians}$$
