Trigonometry

Why Triangles?

An angle is a relational construct.

It exists between two independent things.

Triangles introduce a third thing.

That seems like added complexity! More relations and quantities...

Don't worry, my friend.

There are two concepts for you to master and then it will make sense.

Let me illustrate them for you.

Imagine an angle between two lines of fixed length: 3 (red) and 6 (blue).

The angle could be anything between $0^\circ$ and $180^\circ$. It's completely free to vary.

Practice swinging the little red line around and around until you feel you understand this thoroughly. You can then practice with the big blue line just for fun.

However, if there were another line of fixed length connecting their endpoints, that angle becomes fixed.

Think about it like this: you can only connect three lines of fixed length to form a triangle in one specific way.

For example, if we had three lines with lengths 4, 5 and 6, we could only make them form a triangle with the angles: $41.4^\circ$, $55.8^\circ$, and $82.8^\circ$.

Go ahead and try to make a triangle with anything other than these angles, given these lengths.

And once you have failed at that, you should arrive at the first key realisation: side lengths determine the angle sizes in triangles.

Ah. But wait...

Is the same true in reverse?

With angles fixed at $41.4^\circ$, $55.8^\circ$, and $82.8^\circ$, can we form a triangle with different lengths other than 4, 5 and 6?

Yes — provided the side lengths are scaled proportionally.

The key realisation here is that the ratios between the sides are being preserved, i.e. it is the ratios between sides that determine angles!

So just to hammer those points home once again:

A specific set of side lengths determines a specific set of angles.

A specific set of angles determines specific ratios between side lengths.

Now, why is are these properties so useful?

By themselves, angles are difficult quantities to work with. Triangles turn angles into lengths.

We can compare them, and we can measure them, but there is very little arithmetic we can do with them directly.

Lengths are much more cooperative. They can be measured, added, subtracted, multiplied, and compared with ease.

The purpose of constructing triangles around the actual angle that we might be interested in is therefore all based on this idea of creating dependencies between the side lengths and angles.

Trigonometry exploits this.

Rather than working with angles directly, we convert the problem into one involving lengths and ratios of lengths.

This allows us to calculate unknown angles from measured distances, unknown distances from measured angles, and eventually much more.
