Trigonometry

Overview

"Trigonometry" translates to triangle measurement.

Yet angles are the real subject of interest.

To find an angle between two lines, constructing a triangle is the simplest method. Hence the early emphasis on triangles.

Angles can also be understood as proportions of rotation around a circle; this idea eventually connects trigonometry to oscillations, complex numbers, and Euler's formula.

Typically, I like to move through these explanations conceptually, often with a focus on the historical development of ideas.

However, in this case, I think it's more productive to brute force some of the properties of triangles and angles into your head, and then we can reflect on things conceptually.

Trigonometric Functions

If we know the side lengths of a triangle, we can derive its angles.

For right-angled triangles, we need only know two side lengths.

From the three sides of a right-angled triangle, we can construct six named ratios: sine, cosine, tangent, cosecant, secant, and cotangent.

$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \qquad \cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \qquad \tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}$$

$$ \csc(\theta) = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{opposite}} \qquad \sec(\theta) = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{adjacent}} \qquad \cot(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{opposite}} $$

We can find the mystery angle using whichever ratio is most convenient.

We can change which angle is being considered by redefining which sides are labelled “opposite” and “adjacent.”

Degrees

Let's now think of angles as measuring rotation about a point.

We rotate a line counterclockwise about the center of the circle, beginning with the line pointing to the right along the $x$-axis at $0^\circ$.

Imagine a point where the line crosses the circumference of the circle. As we rotate the line, the point moves around the circumference.

When I say 'about', I am thinking of pinching the line at one end and swivelling it such that, at the other end, the point moves 'around' the circumference of the circle. I hope that clarifies the distinction between 'about' and 'around'.

We have arbitrarily partitioned the circle into 360 parts, which we will call degrees. We will focus on multiples of $30^\circ$ and $45^\circ$, because they work cleanly with equilateral triangles and squares, respectively.

Also, take note of how coordinates are signed depending on quadrant:

$$ \begin{array}{c|c} \text{Quadrant} & \text{Coordinates } (x,y) \\ \hline \text{I} & (+, +) \\ \text{II} & (-, +) \\ \text{III} & (-, -) \\ \text{IV} & (+, -) \end{array} $$

Radians

While degrees arise from an arbitrary partitioning of the circle, radians are intrinsic to the geometry of circles.

Imagine taking the radius of a circle and wrapping it around the circumference, beginning at the $x$-axis (see widget below).

Now connect a line (ray) from the origin to the endpoint of this wrapped radius.

The angle created is defined as 1 radian, which is approximately equivalent to $57.3^\circ$.

How many radii would we need to complete one full rotation around the circle?

The answer is $2\pi$.

Why?

Circumference = $\pi \cdot \text{diameter}$

Diameter = $2r$

Circumference = $2\pi r$

If we have a unit circle, where $r = 1$, then the circumference is just $2\pi$, i.e. $2 \cdot 3.1415 = 6.283$.

If one complete rotation around a circle is $2\pi$ radians, then:

  • half a rotation is $\pi$ radians
  • a quarter rotation is $\frac{\pi}{2}$ radians
  • and so on.
