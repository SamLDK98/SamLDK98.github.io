Trigonometry

1: Angles and Side-Length Ratios

Opening

"Trigonometry" translates to triangle measurement.

Yet angles are the real subject of interest; constructing triangles is just an effective method for working with them.

But why are angles interesting? What even is an angle?

I would highly recommend pausing and thinking about this second question. Learning is only satisfying when driven by curiosity, so make yourself interested in this question. Wrestle with it. Doing so will make it clear where you are unclear, and motivate the search for clarity.

So...

Perhaps you pictured an arc between two lines.

You might think of the arc as representing the distance between both lines.

So why then is it curved? Why not just a straight line?

How exactly is this distance measured?

Does the angle between two lines increase as the distance between them increases?

If you can't quite articulate a crisp, clear definition for what an angle is, don't worry. To quote David Henderson:

"There is no universally agreed definition of an angle. Angles can be conceived of and used in a variety of ways, and while valid definitions may be given for specific contexts, it is difficult to give a single formal definition that is completely satisfactory in capturing all aspects of the general concept of angle."

But don't fret, there are many interesting ways to think about angles. One of my favourites is as fractions of circles. We will get to these later.

Before we get stuck into the slightly flavourless meat of early trigonometry learning, there is one finally point I want to make. Trigonometry leads to an incredibly interesting revelation about how geometry, algebra, and imaginary numbers are part of the same underlying structure. This is captured in Euler's Formula, often cited as "the most beautiful equation in mathematics":

$$e^{i\pi}+1=0$$

Understanding this seemingly unassuming mathematical statement requires only a handful of ideas, and we will get there quickly. I mention it because I want you to be excited by it and to treat it as a north star. However, we must now start with the basic mechanics of angles, triangles, and ratios.

Why do we need triangles to find angles?

An angle is a relational construct.

If you try to picture an angle, you need at least two independent things for the angle to exist between.

Say I'm thinking of an angle between two lines.

I could tell you their lengths:

  • Blue line = 4
  • Yellow line = 5

But there's no way for you to know the size of my angle without more information. It could be anything between $0^\circ$ and $180^\circ$.

If you knew the length of a line connecting the endpoints of these two lines, then you could work out what the angle between them must be.

But why?

Because you can only connect three lines of fixed length to form a triangle in one specific way.

For example, if we had three lines with lengths 4, 5 and 6, we could only make them form a triangle with the angles: $41.4^\circ$, $55.8^\circ$, and $82.8^\circ$.

Go ahead and try to make a triangle with anything other than these angles, given these lengths.

But is the same true in reverse?

With angles fixed at $41.4^\circ$, $55.8^\circ$, and $82.8^\circ$, can we form a triangle with different lengths other than 4, 5 and 6?

Yes — provided the side lengths are scaled proportionally.

The key realisation is that the ratios between the sides are being preserved, i.e. it is the ratios between sides that determine angles!

So:

  • Side lengths determine specific angles.
  • Angles determine specific ratios between side lengths, not absolute lengths.
