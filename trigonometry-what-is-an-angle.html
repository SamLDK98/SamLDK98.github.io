Trigonometry

Measuring Angles

"Trigonometry" translates to triangle measurement.

Yet angles are the real subject of interest.

Angles measure differences between orientations.

This is most simply explained using lines.

A line can be rotated about its midpoint.

Each distinct way the line can be rotated corresponds to an orientation.

The quantity being measured is rotation, not distance.

This can be confusing because in order to represent angles, we must use visualisations that depict some sense of length.

The standard arc shown between two lines might suggest that the length of the arc represents the difference in orientation.

Of course, the length of these arcs is arbitrary.

It may help to imagine a circle partitioned as shown below.

Notice that all of the curved lines cross the same number of partitions regardless of their length or shape.

We can therefore think of an angle as measuring how many such partitions must be crossed to rotate one orientation into another.

Degrees

The familiar degree system is based on a similar idea, partitioning a circle into 360 equal parts.

We will focus on multiplies of $30^\circ$ and $45^\circ$ to mark landmarks around the circle.

Radians

While degrees arise from partitioning a circle into an arbitrary number of parts, there is another unit of angle measurement that emerges directly from the geometry of the circle itself: the radian.

Imagine taking the radius of a circle and wrapping it around the circumference to form an arc starting from $(1,0)$.

The angle formed between the two radii connecting the centre to the endpoints of this arc is defined as 1 radian.

We know that the circumference of a circle is $2\pi r$. So if we let $r = 1$ then the circumference is just $2\pi$.

It therefore takes $2\pi$, or $\approx 6.283$, radians to rotate through one complete revolution.

Note that a radian is not a fraction of $\pi$. Fractions of $\pi$ arise simply because one full rotation happens to be $2\pi$ radians. One radian is approximately $57.3^\circ$, or a little less than $\frac{\pi}{3}$.

So:

  • A full rotation around the circle → $2\pi$
  • Half a rotation → $\pi$
  • Quarter rotation → $\frac{\pi}{2}$
  • Three-quarter rotation → $\frac{3\pi}{2}$

Angles that are multiples of $30^\circ$ are typically expressed as sixths of $\pi$, which can often be reduced to thirds.

Angles that are multiples of $45^\circ$ are typically expressed as fourths of $\pi$.
