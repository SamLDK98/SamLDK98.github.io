Complex Numbers

The Imaginary Unit

The Problem of Negative Square Roots

$\large \sqrt{-9}$

The square root of a number is a number that, when multiplied by itself, produces the original number.

$\large x^2 = -9$

But what could $x$ be here?

Nothing on the real number line works.

$(+) \times (+) = +$

$(-) \times (-) = +$

You might be thinking:

$$3 \times (-3) = -9$$

But:

$$3 \neq -3$$

"Heavens to Betsy! What am $i$ to do?!" you cry, correctly anticipating the next section.

Extending the Number System

This was not the first time a mathematical operation had led to an impossible solution within the existing number system.

$3 - 5= \ ?$

No whole number can answer that. Negative numbers extended the number line to allow:

$$3 - 5 = -2$$

$4 \div 8$

No integer can answer that. Fractions extended the number system to include rational numbers and allow:

$$4 \div 8 = \frac{1}{2}$$

While it was not yet known, the same fate would follow with $\sqrt{-9} = \ ?$.

At first, a new number was created, defined by a single property: this is the number that, when multiplied by itself, gives -1.

$$i= \sqrt{ -1 }$$

Reflecting the skepticism with which they were initially viewed, these quantities became known as imaginary numbers.

The Surprisingly Good Behaviour of $\large i$

When new types of numbers are considered, we have to check if they behave properly with all the other known number types and operations.

Remarkably, introducing $i$ does not break algebra. The familiar laws of commutativity, distributivity, and associativity continue to hold, as do identities and inverses.

The familiar laws of commutativity, distributivity, and associativity continue to hold, as do identities and inverses.
  • Addition is commutative.
  • Multiplication is commutative.
  • Addition is associative.
  • Multiplication is associative.
  • Multiplication distributes over addition.
  • Additive identity ($0$).
  • Multiplicative identity ($1$).
  • Additive inverses ($-z$).
  • Multiplicative inverses ($1/z$, for $z \neq 0$).
Examples of some familiar algebraic behaviours that emerge from these laws for the real numbers also occurring with imaginary numbers.
  1. Like terms can be added and subtracted.
  2. Unlike terms cannot be combined into a single term.
  3. Unlike terms can be multiplied by each other.

$$ \begin{array}{r l c l} \textbf{1.} & 2x + 3x = 5x & \quad & 2\sqrt{-1} + 3\sqrt{-1} = 5\sqrt{-1} \\ \\ \textbf{2.} & 2 + 3x = 2 + 3x & \quad & 2 + 3\sqrt{-1} = 2 + 3\sqrt{-1} \\ \\ \textbf{3.} & 5 \cdot x = 5x & \quad & 5 \cdot \sqrt{-1} = 5\sqrt{-1} \end{array} $$

Besides having a value for $\sqrt{ -1 }$ and gaining solutions to previously unsolvable equations, there are surprisingly few other changes that follow from this.

While the fundamental laws survive, some familiar consequences and function-specific rules do not.

Firstly, ordering is not valid for complex numbers. For complex numbers this has no meaning: $2+3i > 2+i$.

Secondly, the square-root rules require more care.

For non-negative real numbers, we can write:

$$\sqrt{ab}=\sqrt a\,\sqrt b$$

However, this is not always true in the complex numbers.

For example,

$$\sqrt{(-1)(-1)} = \sqrt1 = 1$$

while

$$\sqrt{-1}\,\sqrt{-1} = i^2 = -1$$

Since

$$1 \neq -1$$

the rule has broken down.

Expressing Negative Square Roots in Terms of $i$

Once $i$ had been introduced, negative square roots could be expressed using ordinary real numbers together with $i$.

For example,

$$(5i)^2 = 25i^2 = 25(-1) = -25$$

Therefore,

$$\sqrt{-25}=5i$$

This was important because it meant mathematicians did not need a different symbol for every negative square root.

$$\sqrt{-1}, \quad \sqrt{-2}, \quad \sqrt{-3}, \quad \sqrt{-4}, \dots$$

A single new quantity, $i$, was enough to express them all.

$$\sqrt{-25} = 5i$$

$$\sqrt{-50} = 5\sqrt{2}\,i$$

$$\sqrt{-3} = \sqrt{3}\,i$$

The General Form of a Complex Number

So far, all of our examples have been purely imaginary numbers:

$$5i, \qquad \sqrt{2}\,i, \qquad -3i$$

However, combining a real and imaginary number gives an expression of the form:

$$z=a+bi$$

Numbers of this form are called complex numbers. Here, complex is used in its older sense of composed of parts:

  • A real part ($a$)
  • An imaginary part ($bi$)

Now, if this seems awkward to you—the idea of a single number having independent "parts"—you would be in the company of many of the leading mathematicians of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

However, we will soon see how a two-dimensional number system actually makes a great deal of sense.
