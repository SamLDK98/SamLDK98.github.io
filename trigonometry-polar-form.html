Polar Form

Polar Form

Any position in a two-dimensional space can be represented using $(x,y)$ coordinates.

Any position on the unit circle can also be represented by an angle $\theta$, because the functions sine and cosine map that angle to its corresponding $x$- and $y$-coordinates:

$$(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$$

As $\theta$ varies, these coordinates trace out every point on the unit circle.

The unit circle has a radius of $1$. To reach points elsewhere in the plane, we keep the same angle but extend or shrink the radius.

This can be thought of as first moving around the unit circle to choose a direction, and then moving further away from or closer to the origin along that direction.

If the radius is $r$, then both coordinates scale proportionally:

$$x=r\cos\theta \qquad y=r\sin\theta$$

So any point can be represented as:

$$(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$$

In other words, every point in the plane can be described either by its coordinates $(x,y)$, or by a distance $r$ from the origin and an angle $\theta$.

This is known as polar form.

Complex Numbers

Negative numbers have an interesting geometric interpretation.

Multiplying a number by $-1$ changes its sign. On the number line, this can be visualised as a rotation of $180^\circ$ about the origin.

This raises an interesting question.

If $-1$ corresponds to a rotation of $180^\circ$, might there exist a number corresponding to a rotation of $90^\circ$?

Mathematicians had already encountered a mysterious quantity while solving equations:

$$i=\sqrt{-1}$$

Since

$$i^2=-1,$$

multiplying by $i$ twice has the same effect as multiplying by $-1$: a rotation of $180^\circ$.

This suggests that a single multiplication by $i$ can be interpreted as a rotation of $90^\circ$.

Under this interpretation, multiplying by $i$ rotates numbers off the number line and into a second dimension.

This looks very similar to the Cartesian plane. However, when working with complex numbers we use different terminology:

  • horizontal axis $\rightarrow$ real axis
  • vertical axis $\rightarrow$ imaginary axis

A complex number is written as

$$z=a+bi$$

where:

  • $a$ is the real part
  • $b$ is the imaginary part

Just as the coordinate pair $(a,b)$ identifies a point in the plane, the complex number $a+bi$ identifies the same point in the complex plane.

Polar Form

We can also describe a complex number using a distance and an angle.

If we draw a line from the origin to the point $z=a+bi$, it forms the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle.

Using Pythagoras' theorem, the length of this hypotenuse is

$$r=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}.$$

In the context of complex numbers, this distance from the origin is called the modulus.

We can also describe the point using the angle $\theta$ measured from the positive real axis.

Thus every complex number can be described by:

  • a modulus $r$
  • an angle $\theta$

This is directly analogous to polar coordinates.

So we could describe $z = a+bi$ as $z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$.

How?

The real and imaginary parts can be expressed as:

$$a=r\cos\theta \qquad \text{and} \qquad b=r\sin\theta$$

Substitute these expressions into $z = a +bi$:

$$z=r\cos\theta+i(r\sin\theta)$$

Factor out the $r$:

$$z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$$

This is known as the polar form of a complex number.
