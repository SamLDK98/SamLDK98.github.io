Sine and Cosine as Coordinates

The Circular Nature of Angles

Most types of measurements are linear and are naturally represented on a number line. As they increase in one direction, they decrease in the other.

Angles, however, are naturally represented by a circle. If we continually increase an angle, we eventually return to where we started: $0^\circ = 360^\circ = 720^\circ$.

To move along a circle is to move simultaneously left or right AND up or down.

If we wanted to go from right to left on the $x$-axis -- from $(1,0)$ to $(-1,0)$ -- we can't move purely leftwards. We must also move up or down.

So, as one coordinate changes, the other must change in a way that preserves the circular path.

Sometimes $x$ decreases while $y$ increases, sometimes the reverse, and sometimes they increase or decrease together.

So what does this mean for how we think about angles?

Angles Determine Coordinates

Imagine a line connecting a point on a circle to the origin. As the point moves counterclockwise, an angle ($\theta$) is formed between this radius and the $x$-axis.

Let's set the radius length to 1 to make a unit circle, so the values of each coordinate range from -1 to 1.

If the angle changes, the coordinates of the point change.

In fact, every angle determines a unique pair of coordinates.

But how could we work out what those coordinates are?

Actually, we have actually seen this problem before. We saw how to determine unknown lengths in a right-angled triangle from a known angle.

What happens if we draw such a triangle inside the circle?

The side opposite this angle (green line) has the same $y$-coordinate as the point.

$$\text{opposite side}=y$$

The adjacent side (yellow line) has the same $x$-coordinate as the point.

$$\text{adjacent side}= x$$

The hypotenuse is the radius of the unit circle.

We could therefore use the sine and cosine ratios to work out what the $x$ and $y$ coordinates must be for any given angle ($\theta$).

Recall:

$$ \sin(\theta)=\frac{\text{opposite side}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \qquad \cos(\theta)=\frac{\text{adjacent side}}{\text{hypotenuse}} $$

Notice that our opposite and adjacent sides must range between -1 and 1, because we have seen that they are equal to the $y$ and $x$ coordinates.

Further, since the hypotenuse is just 1, we can read out these values directly.

This means:

$$ \sin(\theta)=y \qquad \cos(\theta)=x $$

This gives us a completely new way to think about sine and cosine.

They are not just ratios between sides of a triangle. On the unit circle, they are the coordinates of a point.

$$(x, y) = (\cos(\theta), \sin(\theta))$$
