Trigonometry

The Unit Circle

The Circular Nature of Angles

Most types of measurements are linear and are naturally represented on a number line. As they increase in one direction, they decrease in the other.

Angles, however, are naturally represented by a circle. If we continually increase an angle, we eventually return to where we started.

We can represent an angle as a point moving around a circle.

As that point moves, it is never moving purely horizontally or purely vertically, but always some combination of both.

If one coordinate changes, the other must change in a way that preserves the circular path. Sometimes $x$ increases while $y$ decreases, sometimes the reverse, and sometimes they increase or decrease together.

So while an angle appears to be a single quantity, its motion around a circle can be represented by two coupled quantities.

Unit Circle

Let's take a moment to understand the unit circle within the context of a coordinate system, and then we will see how this helps with the interpretation of angles.

Every point on the circle has both an $x$-coordinate and a $y$-coordinate. As a point moves around the circle, these coordinates change together in a way that preserves the circular path.

A unit circle has a radius of 1.

Its coordinates therefore range from $-1$ to $1$ on the $x$- and $y$-axes.

The only coordinate values you need to remember are:

$$\frac{1}{2}, \quad \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \quad \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \qquad \to \qquad 0.5, \quad \approx 0.71, \quad \approx 0.87$$

Everything else is just signs and swapping positions.

For coordinates without a '0':

  • Multiples of $45^\circ$ are combinations of $\Large\pm \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$.
  • Multiples of $30^\circ$ are combinations of $\Large \pm \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ and $\large \pm \frac{1}{2}$.

The signs depend on the quadrant:

$$ \begin{array}{c|c} \text{Quadrant} & \text{Coordinates } (x,y) \\ \hline \text{I} & (+, +) \\ \text{II} & (-, +) \\ \text{III} & (-, -) \\ \text{IV} & (+, -) \end{array} $$

Notice how degrees, radians, and coordinates can be three different ways of describing the same point on a circle?

$$90^\circ \qquad \frac{\pi}{2} \qquad (0,1)$$

Make sure you have a good sense of all three before continuing.

Coordinates in Circular Motion

If we graphed the angle as a point moved around the circle, we would see a straight line. As time increases, the angle increases.

(Unless we reset to $0^\circ$ after each revolution. In that case we would see a repeating sawtooth pattern.)

However, what if we tracked the coordinates during a revolution?

Imagine the point beginning at (1,0). What happens to the $x$ and $y$ values during a counterclockwise lap of the circle? Take a moment to think about that.

Initially, $x$ decreases as $y$ increases $(\downarrow, \uparrow)$. After the point crosses the $y$-axis, they both decrease $(\downarrow, \downarrow)$, albeit with $x$ now in the negative numbers.

After passing through the $x$-axis $(-1,0)$, $x$ begins to increase again while $y$ moves down into the negative numbers $(\uparrow, \downarrow)$. Once the point crosses the $y$-axis again, both $x$ and $y$ are then increasing $(\uparrow, \uparrow)$, albeit with $y$ in the negative numbers. Eventually they would reach the starting position again with $x$ at $1$ and $y$ at $0$.

Thinking through that probably doesn't lead to much standing out; however, look at what happens when we graph it, plotting each coordinate against the angle of rotation.

It might seem odd. The angle is changing at a constant rate, yet the coordinates trace out smooth wave patterns.

In other words, uniform motion around a circle produces oscillatory behaviour when viewed through its coordinates.

Sine and Cosine

We have thought about angles in different ways:

  • With respect to right-angled triangles, and the ratios between their side lengths.
  • With respect to circles, as rotations about a point.

It turns out that these are actually the same idea from different perspectives.

Let's see how:

Take any point on the unit circle.

Now draw a vertical line from that point down to the $x$-axis.

This creates a right-angled triangle inside the circle.

The hypotenuse of this triangle is a radius of the circle.

Being a unit circle, the hypotenuse must equal $1$.

This greatly simplifies our trigonometric ratios.

Recall:

$$ \sin(\theta)=\frac{o}{h} \qquad \cos(\theta)=\frac{a}{h} $$

Since:

$$h=1$$

we obtain:

$$ \sin(\theta)=o \qquad \cos(\theta)=a $$

The trigonometric functions no longer give us ratios.

They give us side lengths directly.

But these side lengths are already part of our coordinate system.

The adjacent side is simply the horizontal distance from the origin.

In other words:

$$a=x$$

Likewise, the opposite side is the vertical distance from the origin:

$$o=y$$

So:

$$\cos(\theta)=x$$

and:

$$\sin(\theta)=y$$

The point on the circle corresponding to an angle $\theta$ can therefore be written as:

$$ (x,y) = (\cos(\theta),\sin(\theta)) $$

Every angle determines a point.

Every point determines a pair of coordinates.

And those coordinates are simply the cosine and sine of the angle.

Thinking of Sine and Cosine in Terms of $x$- and $y$-Coordinates

Imagine a line of length 1 extending from the origin.

If the line points directly upward, its endpoint has coordinates $(0,1)$, so its height is 1.

As the line rotates, and becomes slanted, its length remains the same, but its height changes.

Sine tells us what fraction of the line's maximum possible height remains.

For example, if:

$$\sin(\theta)=0.5$$

then the height of the endpoint is half of what it would be if the line were perfectly vertical.

Similarly, cosine tells us what fraction of the line's maximum possible horizontal length remains.

If:

$$\cos(\theta)=0.75$$

then the horizontal position of the endpoint is 75% of what it would be if the line were perfectly horizontal.
